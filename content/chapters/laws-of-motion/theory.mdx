## SECTION 1 — The Big Picture of Newtonian Mechanics

---

### 1.1 What Is Force?

Push a book across a table. Throw a ball. Pull a spring. Watch a magnet attract iron filings. In each case, something is causing a change — in motion, in shape, in speed, in direction.

That "something" is a **force**.

Force is a vector quantity that describes an **interaction between two objects**. It is not a property of a single object — it always involves at least two participants. When you push a wall, the wall pushes back on you with equal magnitude in the opposite direction.

<Callout type="info">
**Force: Working Definition**

A force is an interaction between two objects that can cause acceleration, deformation, or both. Every force has a source object (the one exerting it) and a target object (the one receiving it).
</Callout>

Forces fall into two broad categories:

| Category | Examples | Range |
|----------|----------|-------|
| **Contact forces** | Normal reaction, friction, tension, spring force | Short range (requires physical contact) |
| **Field forces** | Gravity, electrostatic force, magnetic force | Long range (acts through empty space) |

At the fundamental level, all forces in nature reduce to four interactions: gravitational, electromagnetic, strong nuclear, and weak nuclear. In JEE Physics, you work almost entirely with gravity, normal reaction, tension, friction, and spring forces — all of which are either gravitational or electromagnetic at the microscopic level.

---

### 1.2 Kinematics vs Dynamics

**Kinematics** describes *how* objects move: position, velocity, acceleration, displacement. It asks: "What is happening?"

**Dynamics** asks *why*: what forces cause changes in motion. It says: "A net force is required to change velocity."

Newton's Laws are the foundation of dynamics. They connect the forces acting on an object to the resulting acceleration.

---

### 1.3 From Aristotle to Newton — A Historical Journey

**Aristotle (384–322 BCE):** Believed that objects naturally come to rest. A force is required to keep something moving. This seems intuitive — a rolling ball does slow down. But this intuition is wrong because it ignores friction.

**Galileo Galilei (1564–1642):** Performed inclined plane experiments. Realised that on a perfectly smooth surface, a ball would roll indefinitely. The "natural state" is *uniform motion*, not rest. This was revolutionary.

**René Descartes (1596–1650):** Formulated the principle of inertia in words, close to what Newton would later state.

**Isaac Newton (1643–1727):** Published *Principia Mathematica* in 1687. In this single work, he unified Galileo's terrestrial mechanics with Kepler's planetary laws, gave a mathematical framework for force and acceleration, and laid the foundation of classical mechanics that stood unchallenged for 226 years.

<Callout type="tip">
**Why Newton's Laws Transformed Physics**

Before Newton, the heavens and the Earth were governed by separate rules. Newton showed that the same laws that govern a falling apple also govern the Moon's orbit. This unification was one of the greatest intellectual achievements in human history.
</Callout>

---

### 1.4 Limitations of Newtonian Mechanics

Newton's Laws are extraordinarily accurate for everyday situations but break down in three regimes:

| Regime | When it fails | Correct theory |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| Very high speeds | $v \approx c$ (speed of light) | Special Relativity |
| Very small scales | Atomic, subatomic | Quantum Mechanics |
| Very strong gravity | Near black holes | General Relativity |

For JEE, everything operates well within the Newtonian regime. However, understanding these limitations deepens conceptual clarity.

## SECTION 2 — Inertia

---

### 2.1 The Concept of Inertia

Inertia is the **tendency of an object to resist any change in its state of motion**.

It is not a force. It is a *property* — a measure of how stubbornly an object clings to its current state.

Three types of inertia:

**Inertia of Rest:** A body at rest tends to stay at rest.
- *Example:* Passengers jerk backward when a stationary bus suddenly starts. The passengers resist the change from rest to motion.
- *Example:* A coin on a card placed over a glass falls into the glass when the card is flicked away — the coin's inertia keeps it at rest while the card moves.

**Inertia of Motion:** A body in uniform motion tends to continue with the same speed in the same direction.
- *Example:* Passengers jerk forward when a moving bus brakes suddenly. Their bodies resist the change from motion to rest.
- *Example:* A satellite in orbit continues orbiting because there is no friction in space to stop it.

**Inertia of Direction:** A body in motion tends to continue in the same direction.
- *Example:* Passengers tilt outward when a bus turns a corner. Their bodies resist the change in direction.
- *Example:* Water droplets fly tangentially off a spinning wet umbrella.

---

### 2.2 Mass as the Measure of Inertia

Not all objects resist changes equally. A cricket ball and a bowling ball, both at rest, both require force to move — but the bowling ball requires much more.

**Mass is the quantitative measure of inertia.** The greater the mass, the greater the resistance to change in motion.

<Callout type="important">
**Key Distinction: Mass vs Weight**

- **Mass** is the measure of inertia — an intrinsic property. It is a scalar and does not depend on location.
- **Weight** is the gravitational force on an object: $W = mg$. It depends on $g$ and therefore on location.

On the Moon, your mass is the same as on Earth, but your weight is $\dfrac{1}{6}$ of what it is on Earth.
</Callout>

## SECTION 3 — Newton's First Law

---

### 3.1 Statement

<Formula label="Newton's First Law">
An object remains in its state of rest or of uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by a net external force.
</Formula>

Mathematically: If $\sum \vec{F} = \vec{0}$, then $\vec{a} = \vec{0}$ (i.e., $\vec{v}$ = constant).

---

### 3.2 Physical Meaning

The First Law has two profound implications:

1. **Definition of force:** A force is whatever is required to change an object's state of motion. If motion is unchanging, no net force is acting (even if individual forces are present and cancel out).

2. **Existence of inertial frames:** The First Law defines a special class of reference frames — *inertial frames* — in which it holds. An inertial frame is one that is not accelerating.

<Callout type="warning">
**Common Misconception**

Many students think: "A body moving with constant velocity has no force acting on it."

This is **wrong**. A body moving with constant velocity has **no *net* force** acting on it. Many forces may be present — they just cancel. Example: A car moving at constant speed on a level road has engine thrust balanced by friction and air resistance.
</Callout>

---

### 3.3 Inertial Frames of Reference

A **reference frame** is a coordinate system with respect to which motion is measured.

An **inertial frame** is one in which Newton's First Law holds — an object with no net force on it moves in a straight line at constant speed (or stays at rest).

The ground is approximately an inertial frame for most JEE problems (it is actually non-inertial because Earth rotates, but the effect is negligible).

A **non-inertial frame** is an accelerating frame. In such a frame, Newton's laws fail unless a "pseudo force" is introduced (see Section 12).

## SECTION 4 — Newton's Second Law

---

### 4.1 Derivation from Momentum

Linear momentum is defined as:

$$\vec{p} = m\vec{v}$$

Newton's Second Law in its most general form states:

$$\vec{F}_{net} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}$$

For **constant mass**:

$$\vec{F}_{net} = \frac{d(m\vec{v})}{dt} = m\frac{d\vec{v}}{dt} = m\vec{a}$$

<Formula label="Newton's Second Law (constant mass)">
$$\vec{F}_{net} = m\vec{a}$$
</Formula>

<Step n={1}>
Start from momentum: $\vec{p} = m\vec{v}$
</Step>

<Step n={2}>
Differentiate with respect to time: $\dfrac{d\vec{p}}{dt} = \dfrac{d(m\vec{v})}{dt}$
</Step>

<Step n={3}>
For constant mass: $= m\dfrac{d\vec{v}}{dt} = m\vec{a}$
</Step>

<Step n={4}>
Setting equal to net force: $\vec{F}_{net} = m\vec{a}$
</Step>

---

### 4.2 Physical Meaning

- The **net force** (vector sum of all forces) determines acceleration.
- Acceleration is **in the direction of the net force**.
- Doubling the net force doubles the acceleration (for constant mass).
- Doubling the mass halves the acceleration (for constant force).

**Units:** $[F] = [m][a] = \text{kg} \cdot \text{m/s}^2 = \text{Newton (N)}$

**Dimensions:** $[F] = MLT^{-2}$

---

### 4.3 Component Form

Since $\vec{F} = m\vec{a}$ is a vector equation, it holds independently in each direction:

$$F_x = ma_x \qquad F_y = ma_y \qquad F_z = ma_z$$

This allows us to solve 2D and 3D problems by decomposing forces along convenient axes.

---

### 4.4 Variable Mass — Qualitative Discussion

When mass changes with time (rocket ejecting fuel, chain falling on a table), the full form $\vec{F} = \dfrac{d\vec{p}}{dt}$ must be used. For a rocket:

$$F_{thrust} = v_{exhaust} \cdot \left(-\frac{dm}{dt}\right)$$

where $-\dfrac{dm}{dt}$ is the rate of mass ejection.

---

### 4.5 Graphical Interpretation

For a body of constant mass:

- **$F$ vs $a$ graph:** Straight line through origin with slope $m$
- **$a$ vs $\frac{1}{m}$ graph (constant $F$):** Straight line through origin

<Callout type="tip">
**JEE Insight**

The slope of an $F$ vs $a$ graph equals mass. If you see two lines with different slopes on the same graph, the steeper line corresponds to the larger mass.
</Callout>

## SECTION 5 — Momentum

---

### 5.1 Definition and Physical Meaning

<Formula label="Linear Momentum">
$$\vec{p} = m\vec{v}$$
</Formula>

Momentum captures both *how much* mass is moving and *how fast* it is moving. A slow-moving truck and a fast-moving bullet can have the same momentum.

**Units:** kg·m/s = N·s | **Dimensions:** $[p] = MLT^{-1}$ | **Direction:** Same as velocity

---

### 5.2 Momentum vs Kinetic Energy

| Property | Momentum $\vec{p}$ | Kinetic Energy $K$ |
|----------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Type | Vector | Scalar |
| Formula | $m\vec{v}$ | $\dfrac{1}{2}mv^2$ |
| Units | kg·m/s | J |
| Conserved in | All collisions (isolated system) | Only elastic collisions |
| Relation | $K = \dfrac{p^2}{2m}$ | $p = \sqrt{2mK}$ |

---

### 5.3 Conservation of Momentum

<Formula label="Conservation of Linear Momentum">
If $\vec{F}_{net,external} = \vec{0}$, then $\vec{p}_{total} = \text{constant}$

$$m_1\vec{v}_1 + m_2\vec{v}_2 = m_1\vec{v}_1' + m_2\vec{v}_2'$$
</Formula>

This holds for any isolated system regardless of the nature of internal forces — elastic, inelastic, or explosive.

<Callout type="info">
**Why Momentum is Conserved**

From Newton's Third Law: $\vec{F}_{12} = -\vec{F}_{21}$

Internal forces come in equal and opposite pairs. When you sum over the entire system, all internal forces cancel. So the total momentum changes only due to **external** forces.
</Callout>

---

## SECTION 6 — Impulse

---

### 6.1 Derivation

From Newton's Second Law: $\vec{F} = \dfrac{d\vec{p}}{dt}$, so $d\vec{p} = \vec{F}\,dt$

Integrating over time interval $t_1$ to $t_2$:

<Formula label="Impulse-Momentum Theorem">
$$\vec{J} = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \vec{F}\,dt = \Delta\vec{p} = \vec{p}_f - \vec{p}_i$$
</Formula>

For a **constant force**: $\vec{J} = \vec{F} \cdot \Delta t$

---

### 6.2 Force-Time Graph

The **area under an $F$-$t$ graph equals the impulse**:

$$J = \int F\,dt = \text{area under } F\text{-}t \text{ curve}$$

Even if force varies with time, total impulse is the area. A triangular $F$-$t$ pulse of peak $F_0$ over time $\Delta t$ gives $J = \dfrac{1}{2}F_0\Delta t$.

---

### 6.3 Real-Life Applications

**Airbags:** On collision, $\Delta p$ is fixed. Airbag increases $\Delta t$, dramatically reducing peak force $F = \Delta p / \Delta t$.

**Cricket wicket-keeper:** Pulling hands backward increases $\Delta t$, reducing force on hands.

**Landing on foam vs concrete:** Same $\Delta p$, but foam extends $\Delta t$, reducing peak force.

<Callout type="tip">
**JEE Formula**

Average force during impact: $\bar{F} = \dfrac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{m(v_f - v_i)}{\Delta t}$

Be careful with signs — define positive direction first.
</Callout>

## SECTION 7 — Newton's Third Law

---

### 7.1 Statement

<Formula label="Newton's Third Law">
For every action force, there is an equal and opposite reaction force. Forces always occur in pairs.

$$\vec{F}_{A \to B} = -\vec{F}_{B \to A}$$
</Formula>

---

### 7.2 Why Action and Reaction Do NOT Cancel

This is the single most common misconception in the entire chapter.

Action and reaction forces **act on different bodies**. They cannot be added together to find the net force on a single body.

**Example:** A book rests on a table.
- Earth pulls book down with weight $W = mg$ (acts **on book**)
- Book pulls Earth up with $W' = mg$ (Newton's 3rd pair, acts **on Earth**)

These act on **different objects** and do NOT cancel each other.

What cancels the book's weight? The **normal reaction from the table** acting on the book upward. This is NOT a Newton's third law pair — it is a different force entirely.

<Callout type="warning">
**The Classic Trap**

**Wrong:** "Weight and normal reaction are action-reaction pairs."

**Correct:**
- Weight (Earth pulls book) pairs with: book pulling Earth upward.
- Normal reaction (table pushes book) pairs with: book pushing table downward.
- Weight and normal reaction are equal and opposite but act on the SAME body. They balance under Newton's First Law, not Third Law.
</Callout>

---

### 7.3 Identifying Newton's Third Law Pairs

1. They act on **different bodies**
2. **Equal in magnitude**, **opposite in direction**
3. Same **type of force** (both gravitational, or both contact)
4. **Simultaneous** — no time delay

---

### 7.4 Examples

**Walking:** You push ground backward → Ground pushes you forward (this propels you).

**Swimming:** You push water backward → Water pushes you forward.

**Rocket propulsion:** Engine expels gas backward → Gas pushes rocket forward. Works perfectly in vacuum — no air needed.

**Ball bouncing:** Ball pushes floor down → Floor pushes ball up (causing bounce).

## SECTION 8 — Free Body Diagrams

---

### 8.1 What Is a Free Body Diagram?

A free body diagram (FBD) is an isolated diagram of a single body showing **all forces acting on that body only**. It is the most essential tool for applying Newton's laws.

---

### 8.2 Systematic Method

<Step n={1}>
**Isolate the body.** Draw the object separated from its surroundings (as a dot or box).
</Step>

<Step n={2}>
**Identify every force ON that body.** Check for: gravity, normal force, tension, friction, applied force, spring force. Include only forces acting on this body.
</Step>

<Step n={3}>
**Draw force vectors** from the body in correct directions, labelled with symbols ($W$, $N$, $T$, $f$, etc.).
</Step>

<Step n={4}>
**Choose coordinate axes.** For inclined problems, tilt axes along and perpendicular to the incline.
</Step>

<Step n={5}>
**Resolve all forces** into components along each axis.
</Step>

<Step n={6}>
**Apply Newton's Second Law** along each axis: $\sum F_x = ma_x$, $\sum F_y = ma_y$.
</Step>

## SECTION 9 — Common Forces

---

### 9.1 Normal Reaction

**Origin:** Electromagnetic repulsion between atoms at the contact interface.

**Direction:** Always **perpendicular to the contact surface**, pointing away from the surface into the body. It can only push, never pull.

**Magnitude:** Determined by Newton's Second Law — it adjusts automatically.

<Callout type="warning">
**$N = mg$ is NOT always true**

| Situation | Normal Force |
|-----------|-------------|
| Horizontal surface, no vertical acceleration | $N = mg$ |
| Inclined plane (angle $\theta$) | $N = mg\cos\theta$ |
| Elevator accelerating upward at $a$ | $N = m(g+a)$ |
| Elevator accelerating downward at $a$ | $N = m(g-a)$ |
| Force $F$ applied at angle $\alpha$ above horizontal | $N = mg - F\sin\alpha$ |
</Callout>

---

### 9.2 Tension

**Definition:** Force transmitted through a taut string, rope, or cable.

**Ideal String Assumptions:**
1. Massless (unless stated otherwise)
2. Inextensible (does not stretch)
3. Always taut

For a **massless string**, tension is uniform throughout. (Any segment has $m = 0$, so net force = 0 → tension same at both ends.)

For a **massive string** (mass $M$, length $L$) being pulled with force $F$:

At distance $x$ from the free end:

$$T(x) = \frac{F \cdot x}{L} \quad \text{(linear variation)}$$

Tension is maximum at the pulled end ($= F$) and zero at the free end.

---

### 9.3 Spring Force — Hooke's Law

<Formula label="Hooke's Law">
$$F_{spring} = kx$$
</Formula>

where $k$ = spring constant (N/m), $x$ = deformation from natural length.

The spring **opposes** deformation — it pulls back if stretched, pushes back if compressed.

| Configuration | Effective Spring Constant |
|---------------|--------------------------|
| Springs in series | $\dfrac{1}{k_{eff}} = \dfrac{1}{k_1} + \dfrac{1}{k_2}$ |
| Springs in parallel | $k_{eff} = k_1 + k_2$ |

## SECTION 10 — Friction

---

### 10.1 Origin of Friction

Friction arises from electromagnetic interactions between atoms at the contact interface. Even apparently smooth surfaces have microscopic asperities (bumps) that interlock. Breaking these interlocks requires force.

---

### 10.2 Types of Friction

**Static Friction ($f_s$):** Acts when there is no relative sliding. Self-adjusting:

$$0 \leq f_s \leq \mu_s N$$

**Limiting (Maximum) Static Friction:** $f_{s,max} = \mu_s N$

**Kinetic Friction ($f_k$):** Acts during sliding:

$$f_k = \mu_k N$$

where $\mu_k < \mu_s$ always.

---

### 10.4 Direction of Friction

Friction always opposes **relative motion** (kinetic) or **tendency of relative motion** (static).

<Callout type="warning">
**Most Common Friction Mistake**

Friction opposes relative motion between the two surfaces in contact — NOT the absolute motion of the body.

Example: A block placed on an accelerating conveyor belt. If the belt moves faster than the block, friction on the block points **forward** (in the direction of belt motion) to accelerate the block to match belt speed.
</Callout>

---

### 10.5 Angle of Friction and Angle of Repose

**Angle of friction $\lambda$:** $\tan\lambda = \mu_s$

**Angle of repose $\theta_r$:** Maximum incline angle at which block stays without sliding.

$$\tan\theta_r = \mu_s$$

Note: angle of friction = angle of repose. If $\theta > \theta_r$, block slides.

## SECTION 11 — Applications of Newton's Laws

---

### 11.1 Connected Blocks — Horizontal Surface

**Two blocks $m_1$ and $m_2$, force $F$ on $m_1$, frictionless:**

$$a = \frac{F}{m_1 + m_2}$$

$$T = \frac{m_2 F}{m_1 + m_2}$$

**With kinetic friction $\mu_k$:**

$$a = \frac{F - \mu_k(m_1+m_2)g}{m_1+m_2}$$

**FBD of $m_1$ (down positive):** $m_1 g - T = m_1 a$

**FBD of $m_2$ (up positive):** $T - m_2 g = m_2 a$

Adding both equations:

<Formula label="Atwood Machine">
$$a = \frac{(m_1 - m_2)g}{m_1 + m_2} \qquad T = \frac{2m_1 m_2 g}{m_1 + m_2}$$
</Formula>

<Callout type="info">
**Limiting Cases**

| Condition | Result |
|-----------|--------|
| $m_1 = m_2$ | $a = 0$, $T = mg$ |
| $m_2 \to 0$ | $a \to g$, $T \to 0$ (free fall) |
| $m_1 \gg m_2$ | $a \to g$, $T \to 2m_2 g$ |
</Callout>

### 11.4 Inclined Plane

**Smooth incline (block slides down):**

$$a = g\sin\theta \qquad N = mg\cos\theta$$

**Rough incline (block slides down):**

$$a = g(\sin\theta - \mu_k\cos\theta) \qquad N = mg\cos\theta$$

**Condition for block to stay stationary on rough incline:**

$$\tan\theta \leq \mu_s$$

---

### 11.5 Pulley Systems — Constraint Method

**Fixed pulley (massless, frictionless):** Changes direction of string only. Tension same on both sides. Speed of both string ends equal.

**Movable pulley:** Both string segments support the pulley load. If movable pulley (with mass $M$) is in equilibrium: $2T = Mg$.

**Constraint relation for movable pulley:**

If movable pulley moves down by $x$, each string segment on either side shortens by $x$ → the free end of string moves $2x$.

$$\boxed{a_{block} = 2a_{pulley}}$$

### 11.6 Wedge Problems

For a block (mass $m$) on a frictionless wedge (mass $M$, angle $\theta$) on a frictionless floor:

The normal force $N$ from wedge on block acts perpendicular to the incline surface.

<Formula label="Wedge Acceleration">
$$a_{wedge} = \frac{mg\sin\theta\cos\theta}{M + m\sin^2\theta}$$
</Formula>

**Strategy:** Use constraint (block stays on wedge surface) to relate accelerations, then apply Newton's laws to each body separately.

## SECTION 12 — Non-Inertial Frames and Pseudo Force

---

### 12.1 Why Newton's Laws Fail in Accelerating Frames

Inside an accelerating car, a ball on the seat appears to accelerate backward with no force pushing it. Newton's First Law seems violated. The issue: you are in a **non-inertial frame**.

---

### 12.2 Pseudo Force

To apply Newton's laws in a non-inertial frame, add a **pseudo force** to every object:

<Formula label="Pseudo Force">
$$\vec{F}_{pseudo} = -m\vec{a}_{frame}$$
</Formula>

Properties:
- **Not a real force** (no physical agent exerts it)
- Acts on every mass in the non-inertial frame
- Always **opposite** to the frame's acceleration
- **Proportional to mass** of the object

---

### 12.3 Elevator via Pseudo Force

Elevator accelerating upward at $a$. In elevator's frame, apply pseudo force $ma$ downward on person:

$$N - mg - ma = 0 \Rightarrow N = m(g+a) \checkmark$$

Same result as ground frame — as it should be.

---

### 12.4 Block on Accelerating Wedge

Block on wedge; wedge accelerates at $a$ horizontally. In wedge frame, pseudo force $ma$ acts horizontally (backward) on block.

Resolving along incline (taking down-incline as positive):

$$ma_{rel} = mg\sin\theta - ma\cos\theta$$

The block's acceleration relative to wedge: $a_{rel} = g\sin\theta - a\cos\theta$

<Callout type="tip">
**Frame Selection Guide**

- **Ground (inertial) frame:** Use when you need absolute velocities/positions or when nothing is at rest in a moving frame.
- **Non-inertial (body) frame:** Use when a body is stationary relative to the accelerating frame — then pseudo force replaces the acceleration and equilibrium equations apply.
</Callout>

## SECTION 13 — Advanced JEE Insights

---

### 13.1 Constraint Relations — Systematic Method

<Step n={1}>
**Identify all movable parts** — each block, each pulley that can move.
</Step>

<Step n={2}>
**Write the total string length** (constant) as a sum of distances measured from fixed reference points.
</Step>

<Step n={3}>
**Differentiate once** to get velocity relations.
</Step>

<Step n={4}>
**Differentiate again** to get acceleration relations.
</Step>

**Example — Double Atwood Machine:**

Fixed pulley P₁ at ceiling. String over P₁: left side has $m_1$, right side has movable pulley P₂. String over P₂: $m_2$ on one side, $m_3$ on the other.

Let $y_1$, $y_2$, $y_3$ = positions of $m_1$, $m_2$, $m_3$ below ceiling; $y_{P2}$ = position of P₂.

Constraint 1 (string over P₁): $y_1 + y_{P2} = L_1$ → $\dot{y}_1 + \dot{y}_{P2} = 0$ → $a_1 + a_{P2} = 0$

Constraint 2 (string over P₂): $(y_2 - y_{P2}) + (y_3 - y_{P2}) = L_2$ → $a_2 + a_3 = 2a_{P2}$

From constraint 1: $a_{P2} = -a_1$, so $a_2 + a_3 = -2a_1$.

---

### 13.2 Hidden Assumptions in Pulley Problems

1. **Massless frictionless pulley:** Tension same on both sides. If pulley has mass, use torque equations.
2. **Inextensible string:** Length constraint holds exactly.
3. **String stays taut:** Verify $T > 0$. If $T < 0$, string went slack.
4. **Surface is rigid:** Normal force $N \geq 0$. If $N < 0$, object left the surface.

---

### 13.3 Symmetry Arguments

- Two identical masses in symmetric Atwood: $a = 0$ by symmetry, $T = mg$.
- Force between equal masses connected symmetrically: both accelerate equally.
- A symmetric structure (equal forces at equal angles) has zero net horizontal force.

Always check symmetry before computing.

---

### 13.4 Choosing the Best Frame

| Scenario | Best Frame |
|----------|-----------|
| Absolute velocity/position needed | Ground (inertial) |
| Block stationary on accelerating truck | Truck frame + pseudo force |
| Block sliding on accelerating wedge | Ground frame (constraint + Newton's laws) |
| Rotating system | Rotating frame + pseudo (centrifugal) force |

---

### 13.5 Limiting Case Verification

After solving, always verify:

| Limit | Expected result |
|-------|----------------|
| $\theta \to 0$ (flat) | Incline formula → flat surface formula |
| $m_2 \to 0$ (Atwood) | $a \to g$ |
| $\mu \to 0$ (smooth) | Friction terms vanish |
| $M \to \infty$ (massive object) | Acceleration $\to 0$ |

## SECTION 14 — Complete Error Analysis

---

| # | Mistake | Correct Approach |
|---|---------|-----------------|
| 1 | Drawing forces exerted BY the body in its own FBD | Include only forces exerted ON the body |
| 2 | Assuming $N = mg$ always | $N$ is determined by Newton's 2nd Law each time |
| 3 | Treating weight and normal reaction as Newton's 3rd pair | They are NOT — they act on the same body |
| 4 | Adding accelerations of different bodies without constraint check | Use constraint equations to relate accelerations |
| 5 | Forgetting friction on incline when "smooth" is not stated | Always read problem statement carefully |
| 6 | Using $f_k = \mu_k mg$ on an incline | Use $f_k = \mu_k N = \mu_k mg\cos\theta$ |
| 7 | Assuming tension is same through a massive pulley | True only for massless frictionless pulley |
| 8 | Forgetting pseudo force in non-inertial frame | Add $-m\vec{a}_{frame}$ to every object in the frame |
| 9 | Thinking static friction is always zero for stationary objects | Static friction is zero only when no horizontal force is applied |
| 10 | Applying momentum conservation with external forces present | Check: net external force = 0 is required |
| 11 | Thinking action-reaction forces cancel | They act on different bodies and never cancel for a single body |
| 12 | Sign error in Atwood machine | Define positive direction consistently for each FBD (down for heavier, up for lighter) |
| 13 | Forgetting to check if block actually slides before using $f_k$ | First check if $F_{applied} > f_{s,max}$; if not, block is stationary |
| 14 | Applying $F = ma$ to entire system when parts have different accelerations | Use individual FBDs for each body |

## SECTION 15 — Solved Examples

### JEE Main Level Examples

**M1.** A 5 kg block on a frictionless surface has 20 N applied horizontally. Find acceleration.

**Solution:** $a = F/m = 20/5 = \mathbf{4 \ m/s^2}$

---

**M2.** A 10 kg block on a horizontal surface ($\mu_s = 0.4$, $\mu_k = 0.3$, $g = 10$ m/s²). Applied force = 30 N horizontal. Find friction and acceleration.

**Solution:**
$f_{s,max} = 0.4 \times 10 \times 10 = 40$ N. Since $30 < 40$, block does not move. $f_s = 30$ N, $a = 0$.

---

**M3.** Repeat M2 with 50 N applied.

**Solution:**
$50 > 40$ N → block slides. $f_k = 0.3 \times 100 = 30$ N.
$$a = \frac{50 - 30}{10} = \mathbf{2 \ m/s^2}$$

---

**M4.** Blocks 3 kg and 5 kg connected on frictionless surface, 24 N on 5 kg block. Find $a$ and $T$.

**Solution:**
$a = 24/8 = 3$ m/s². FBD of 3 kg: $T = 3 \times 3 = \mathbf{9 \ N}$.

---

**M5.** Block 4 kg on smooth 30° incline. Find $a$ and $N$. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
$a = g\sin 30° = 5$ m/s². $N = 4 \times 10 \times \cos 30° = 20\sqrt{3} \approx \mathbf{34.6 \ N}$.

---

**M6.** 60 kg person in elevator accelerating upward at 2 m/s². Apparent weight?

**Solution:**
$N = 60(10 + 2) = \mathbf{720 \ N}$

---

**M7.** 2 kg body moving at 10 m/s brought to rest in 0.5 s. Find braking force.

**Solution:**
$F = \Delta p / \Delta t = 2 \times 10 / 0.5 = \mathbf{40 \ N}$

---

**M8.** Atwood: $m_1 = 8$ kg, $m_2 = 4$ kg. Find $a$ and $T$.

**Solution:**
$$a = \frac{(8-4) \times 10}{12} = \frac{10}{3} \approx \mathbf{3.33 \ m/s^2}$$
$$T = \frac{2 \times 8 \times 4 \times 10}{12} \approx \mathbf{53.3 \ N}$$

---

**M9.** Block 3 kg on rough surface ($\mu_k = 0.25$), 15 N applied. Find $a$.

**Solution:**
$f_k = 0.25 \times 3 \times 10 = 7.5$ N. $a = (15 - 7.5)/3 = \mathbf{2.5 \ m/s^2}$

---

**M10.** Bullet 10 g, 500 m/s enters wall, exits at 100 m/s in 0.01 s. Average resistive force?

**Solution:**
$\Delta p = 0.01(100 - 500) = -4$ kg·m/s. $F = -4/0.01 = \mathbf{400 \ N}$

---

**M11.** Block 5 kg on rough incline (37°, $\mu_k = 0.3$) slides down. Find $a$. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
$N = 5 \times 10 \times 0.8 = 40$ N. $f_k = 12$ N. $mg\sin 37° = 30$ N.
$$a = \frac{30 - 12}{5} = \mathbf{3.6 \ m/s^2}$$

---

**M12.** Block A (4 kg) on frictionless table, string over pulley to hanging block B (2 kg). Find $a$ and $T$.

**Solution:**
$m_B g - T = m_B a$ → $20 - T = 2a$ ...(i)
$T = m_A a = 4a$ ...(ii)
Adding: $20 = 6a$ → $a = 10/3$ m/s². $T = 40/3 \approx \mathbf{13.3 \ N}$

---

**M13.** Ball (0.5 kg): before impact $-20$ m/s, after $+30$ m/s, contact 0.01 s. Average force?

**Solution:**
$\Delta p = 0.5(30 - (-20)) = 25$ N·s. $F = 25/0.01 = \mathbf{2500 \ N}$

---

**M14.** Block 5 kg suspended by two strings at 30° and 60° with vertical. Find tensions.

**Solution:**
Horizontal: $T_1\sin 30° = T_2\sin 60°$ → $T_1 = T_2\sqrt{3}$

Vertical: $T_1\cos 30° + T_2\cos 60° = 50$

Substituting: $T_2\sqrt{3} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} + T_2 \cdot \frac{1}{2} = 50$ → $2T_2 = 50$ → $T_2 = 25$ N, $T_1 = 25\sqrt{3} \approx \mathbf{43.3 \ N}$

---

**M15.** Block 2 kg pulled on rough surface ($\mu_k = 0.3$) by force 10 N at 30° above horizontal. Find $a$. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
$F_y = 10\sin 30° = 5$ N (up). $N = 20 - 5 = 15$ N. $f_k = 4.5$ N.
$a = (10\cos 30° - 4.5)/2 = (8.66 - 4.5)/2 \approx \mathbf{2.08 \ m/s^2}$

---

**M16.** Three blocks A (2 kg), B (3 kg), C (5 kg) in contact on frictionless surface, 50 N on A. Find contact forces.

**Solution:**
$a = 50/10 = 5$ m/s². Force on C = $5 \times 5 = 25$ N. Force on (B+C) from A = $(3+5) \times 5 = \mathbf{40 \ N}$

---

**M17.** 2 kg particle: force $F = 3t^2$ N. Impulse in first 2 s. Find velocity at $t = 2$ s.

**Solution:**
$J = \int_0^2 3t^2\, dt = [t^3]_0^2 = 8$ N·s. The body starts from rest, so $\Delta p = J$ gives $v = J/m = 8/2 = \mathbf{4 \ m/s}$.

---

**M18.** 1200 kg car at 20 m/s stopped in 5 s. Braking force?

**Solution:**
$a = (0 - 20)/5 = -4$ m/s². $F = 1200 \times 4 = \mathbf{4800 \ N}$

---

**M19.** Box (5 kg) in elevator decelerating at 2 m/s² while going up. Apparent weight?

**Solution:**
Decelerating upward = accelerating downward. $N = 5(10 - 2) = \mathbf{40 \ N}$

---

**M20.** Blocks 2 kg and 3 kg connected by string on rough incline (37°, $\mu_k = 0.2$) and hanging pulley. 3 kg hangs, 2 kg on incline. Find $a$. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
$N = 2 \times 10 \times 0.8 = 16$ N. $f_k = 0.2 \times 16 = 3.2$ N.

FBD 3 kg: $30 - T = 3a$ ...(i)

FBD 2 kg (up incline): $T - 2 \times 10 \times 0.6 - 3.2 = 2a$

$T - 12 - 3.2 = 2a$ → $T - 15.2 = 2a$ ...(ii)

Adding: $14.8 = 5a$ → $a \approx \mathbf{2.96 \ m/s^2}$

### JEE Advanced Level Examples

---

**A1.** Block A (mass $m$) on rough surface ($\mu$). Block B (mass $m$) on top. Force $F$ applied to A. Find max $F$ for them to move together.

**Solution:**
Together: $a = F/(2m)$

For B not to slip on A: $f_s = ma = F/2 \leq \mu mg$

$$F_{max} = 2\mu mg$$

If $F > 2\mu mg$: B has $a_B = \mu g$, A has $a_A = (F - \mu mg)/m$.

---

**A2.** Chain (mass $M$, length $L$) on rough table ($\mu$). Maximum hanging fraction without sliding?

**Solution:**
Hanging fraction $f$, hanging mass $= fM$.

For equilibrium: $fMg \leq \mu(1-f)Mg$

$$f_{max} = \frac{\mu}{1 + \mu}$$

---

**A3.** Block on rough incline (angle $\theta$, $\mu$). Horizontal force $P$ applied. Find $P$ to just move block up.

**Solution:**
When about to move up, friction acts down.

$N = mg\cos\theta + P\sin\theta$

Along incline: $P\cos\theta = mg\sin\theta + \mu N = mg\sin\theta + \mu(mg\cos\theta + P\sin\theta)$

$$P = \frac{mg(\sin\theta + \mu\cos\theta)}{\cos\theta - \mu\sin\theta} = mg\tan(\theta + \lambda)$$

where $\lambda = \arctan\mu$ is the angle of friction.

---

**A4.** Block on rough incline ($\theta$, $\mu$). Force $P$ at angle $\phi$ above incline. Find $\phi$ that minimises $P$ to just move block up.

**Solution:**
$N = mg\cos\theta - P\sin\phi$

Along incline: $P\cos\phi = mg\sin\theta + \mu N = mg\sin\theta + \mu(mg\cos\theta - P\sin\phi)$

$P(\cos\phi + \mu\sin\phi) = mg(\sin\theta + \mu\cos\theta)$

$$P = \frac{mg(\sin\theta + \mu\cos\theta)}{\cos\phi + \mu\sin\phi}$$

To minimise $P$, maximise denominator $\cos\phi + \mu\sin\phi$:

$\frac{d}{d\phi}(\cos\phi + \mu\sin\phi) = 0 \Rightarrow -\sin\phi + \mu\cos\phi = 0$

$$\boxed{\phi = \arctan\mu = \lambda}$$

Minimum force is applied at angle equal to the angle of friction above the incline.

---

**A5.** Double Atwood: fixed pulley P₁, hanging pulley P₂ (massless). Over P₂: $m_2$ and $m_3$. Over P₁: $m_1$ (left), P₂ (right). Find accelerations.

**Solution:**
Take upward as positive. Let $T_2$ be the tension in the string over the movable pulley P₂. Since P₂ is massless, the string over the fixed pulley P₁ has tension $T_1 = 2T_2$.

**Constraints.** Over P₁, if $m_1$ accelerates up at $a_1$, then P₂ accelerates down at $a_1$. Let $a_0$ be the acceleration of $m_2$ relative to P₂; then $m_3$ has relative acceleration $-a_0$. The absolute accelerations are:

$$a_{m_2} = -a_1 + a_0, \qquad a_{m_3} = -a_1 - a_0$$

**Newton's second law** for each mass:

$$2T_2 - m_1 g = m_1 a_1 \quad (1)$$
$$T_2 - m_2 g = m_2(-a_1 + a_0) \quad (2)$$
$$T_2 - m_3 g = m_3(-a_1 - a_0) \quad (3)$$

Eliminating $T_2$ and $a_0$ from these three equations gives the acceleration of $m_1$:

$$a_1 = g\cdot\frac{m_1(m_2+m_3) - 4m_2m_3}{m_1(m_2+m_3) + 4m_2m_3}$$

---

**A6.** Block A (2m) on smooth floor, Block B (m) on top of A ($\mu$ between A–B). Force $F$ on A (forward), force $F$ on B (backward, opposite). Find $a_A$ and $a_B$.

**Solution:**
Take the forward direction (direction of $F$ on A) as positive. The force $F$ on B points backward, so B tends to slide backward relative to A; kinetic friction on B from A therefore acts **forward**, and its reaction on A acts **backward**. With $N = mg$, the friction magnitude when slipping is $f = \mu mg$.

**Block B** (forces: $-F$ from the applied force, $+f$ from friction):

$$-F + \mu mg = m\,a_B \quad\Rightarrow\quad a_B = \mu g - \frac{F}{m}$$

**Block A** (forces: $+F$ applied, $-f$ friction reaction):

$$F - \mu mg = 2m\,a_A \quad\Rightarrow\quad a_A = \frac{F - \mu mg}{2m}$$

**Consistency check:** for $F/m > \mu g$ we get $a_A > a_B$, so B does slide backward relative to A — confirming friction on B acts forward, as assumed.

---

**A7.** Find the tension in a rope of mass $m$ and length $L$ hanging from the ceiling, at a point distance $x$ from the ceiling.

**Solution:**
Mass below point at distance $x$ from ceiling = $\dfrac{L - x}{L} \cdot m$

This mass hangs in equilibrium, supported by tension $T$:

$$T = \frac{(L-x)}{L} \cdot mg = mg\left(1 - \frac{x}{L}\right)$$

At $x = 0$ (ceiling): $T = mg$ ✓. At $x = L$ (bottom): $T = 0$ ✓

---

**A8.** A block (mass $M$) is on a wedge (mass $m$, angle $\theta$) on a smooth floor. All surfaces frictionless. Find the normal force between block and wedge.

**Solution:**
Let $a_w$ = wedge acceleration (rightward), $a_{rel}$ = block's acceleration relative to wedge (down the incline).

Block horizontal equation: $-N\sin\theta = M(a_w - a_{rel}\cos\theta)$ ...(1)

Block vertical equation: $N\cos\theta - Mg = M(-a_{rel}\sin\theta)$ ...(2)

Wedge horizontal: $N\sin\theta = ma_w$ ...(3)

From (3): $a_w = N\sin\theta/m$ ...(4)

From (2): $a_{rel} = \frac{Mg - N\cos\theta}{M\sin\theta}$ ...(5)

Substitute (4) and (5) into (1):

$$-N\sin\theta = M\left(\frac{N\sin\theta}{m} - \frac{(Mg - N\cos\theta)\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}\right)$$

After algebra:

Using $(M+m)\sin^2\theta + m\cos^2\theta = M\sin^2\theta + m$, this simplifies to:

$$\boxed{N = \frac{Mmg\sin\theta\cos\theta}{M\sin^2\theta + m}}$$

---

**A9.** A truck accelerates at $a$ m/s². A box (mass $m$) is on the truck floor ($\mu_s$). What is the maximum $a$ such that the box doesn't slip?

**Solution:**
In ground frame: friction from floor on box provides box's acceleration.

For box not to slip: $f_s \leq \mu_s mg$

$f_s = ma$ (required friction)

Condition: $ma \leq \mu_s mg$

$$a_{max} = \mu_s g$$

---

**A10.** Block of mass $m$ is on a rough incline ($\theta$, $\mu_s$). Find the range of horizontal force $P$ (applied to the block) for the block to remain in equilibrium.

**Solution:**
**Case 1: Block about to slide up** (friction acts down the incline)

$N = mg\cos\theta + P\sin\theta$

$P\cos\theta = mg\sin\theta + \mu_s(mg\cos\theta + P\sin\theta)$

$P(\cos\theta - \mu_s\sin\theta) = mg(\sin\theta + \mu_s\cos\theta)$

$P_{max} = mg\tan(\theta + \lambda)$

**Case 2: Block about to slide down** (friction acts up the incline)

$P\cos\theta + \mu_s N = mg\sin\theta$

$P(\cos\theta + \mu_s\sin\theta) = mg(\sin\theta - \mu_s\cos\theta)$

$P_{min} = mg\tan(\theta - \lambda)$ (only valid if $\theta > \lambda$; otherwise $P_{min} = 0$)

**Range of $P$:**

$$mg\tan(\theta - \lambda) \leq P \leq mg\tan(\theta + \lambda)$$

### Complete Formula Sheet

<Formula label="Newton's Laws — Master Formula Sheet">

**Newton's First Law:** $\sum \vec{F} = 0 \Leftrightarrow \vec{a} = 0$

**Newton's Second Law:** $\vec{F}_{net} = m\vec{a} = \dfrac{d\vec{p}}{dt}$

**Newton's Third Law:** $\vec{F}_{AB} = -\vec{F}_{BA}$

**Linear Momentum:** $\vec{p} = m\vec{v}$ ; dimensions $[MLT^{-1}]$

**Impulse:** $\vec{J} = \Delta\vec{p} = \vec{F}_{avg}\Delta t = \displaystyle\int F\,dt$

**Conservation of Momentum:** $\vec{F}_{ext} = 0 \Rightarrow \sum m_i\vec{v}_i = \text{const}$

**Friction:** $f_s \leq \mu_s N$ ; $f_k = \mu_k N$ ; $\mu_k < \mu_s$

**Angle of repose:** $\tan\theta_r = \mu_s$

**Inclined plane:** $a = g(\sin\theta \mp \mu_k\cos\theta)$ ; $N = mg\cos\theta$

**Atwood machine:** $a = \dfrac{(m_1 - m_2)g}{m_1 + m_2}$ ; $T = \dfrac{2m_1m_2g}{m_1+m_2}$

**Elevator (upward $a$):** $N = m(g+a)$ ; **(downward $a$):** $N = m(g-a)$

**Pseudo force:** $\vec{F}_{pseudo} = -m\vec{a}_{frame}$

**Wedge acceleration (frictionless):** $a_W = \dfrac{mg\sin\theta\cos\theta}{M + m\sin^2\theta}$

**Hooke's Law:** $F = kx$

**Massive string tension at distance $x$ from pulled end:** $T(x) = \dfrac{F \cdot x}{L}$

</Formula>

### Last-Minute Revision Tips

<Callout type="tip">
**5 Golden Rules — Newton's Laws**

1. **Always draw FBDs** — one for each body. Never mix forces from different bodies.

2. **Choose axes wisely** — along the incline (not horizontal/vertical) saves enormous calculation.

3. **State Newton's 2nd Law explicitly** — write $\sum F = ma$ for each axis before substituting numbers.

4. **Check limiting cases** — verify your formula with $\theta = 0$, $m = 0$, or $\mu = 0$.

5. **Verify string tension is positive** — if $T < 0$, the string is slack and the setup changes.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
**3 Traps That Kill Marks in Exam**

1. **Atwood tension trap:** Tension $T$ is NOT equal to $m_1g$ or $m_2g$. It lies between them: $m_2g < T < m_1g$.

2. **Friction direction trap:** On a rough incline with an applied force, friction can be up or down the incline. Determine which way the block *tends* to move, then oppose that tendency.

3. **Non-inertial frame trap:** Pseudo force acts on EVERY mass in the frame — including objects at rest relative to the frame. Don't forget it even for stationary objects.
</Callout>

<Callout type="info">
**Concept Connections**

Newton's Laws connect to almost every chapter in mechanics:
- **Work-Energy Theorem:** Derived directly from $F = ma$
- **Circular Motion:** Centripetal force is net force toward center ($F_{net} = mv^2/r$)
- **Rotational Mechanics:** Torque is the rotational analogue of force ($\tau = I\alpha$)
- **Gravitation:** $mg$ is the gravitational force; weightlessness when in free fall
- **SHM:** Restoring force $F = -kx$ is Newton's 2nd Law applied to a spring
</Callout>
