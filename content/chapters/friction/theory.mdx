## SECTION 1 — The Big Picture of Friction

---

### 1.1 What Is Friction?

Run your finger across sandpaper — you feel resistance. Push a heavy box across the floor — you need sustained effort just to keep it moving. Tires grip the road, screws hold in wood, chalk marks a blackboard. All of these phenomena share one root cause: **friction**.

Friction is the **force that opposes relative motion or tendency of relative motion** between two surfaces in contact.

Without friction:
- You could not walk (your feet would slip backward)
- Cars could not accelerate or brake
- Screws, nails, and bolts would be useless
- No musical instrument would make sound by bowing

Yet friction also wastes energy, wears down machinery, and generates unwanted heat. Understanding it precisely — not just "friction opposes motion" — is what separates a JEE topper from an average student.

---

### 1.2 Microscopic Origin

Even surfaces that look smooth are microscopically rough. Zoom in on any surface and you see peaks (asperities) and valleys. When two surfaces are pressed together:

1. Asperities interlock at the contact points
2. Real contact area is far smaller than apparent area
3. Breaking these interlocks requires force

At a deeper level, friction is **electromagnetic in origin** — the repulsion between electron clouds of atoms at the contact surface.

<Callout type="info">
**Remarkable Fact**

The actual contact area between two surfaces is typically less than 1% of their apparent geometric area, even for surfaces that appear perfectly flat. This is why real contact force (and therefore friction) depends on normal force, not on apparent area.
</Callout>

---

### 1.3 Types of Friction

| Type | When it acts | Formula | Direction |
|------|-------------|---------|-----------|
| **Static friction** | No relative sliding | $0 \leq f_s \leq \mu_s N$ | Opposes tendency of motion |
| **Kinetic (sliding) friction** | During relative sliding | $f_k = \mu_k N$ | Opposes relative motion |
| **Rolling friction** | During rolling | $f_r = \mu_r N$ ($\mu_r \ll \mu_k$) | Opposes rolling direction |
| **Fluid friction** | In fluids (viscosity) | $F = 6\pi\eta r v$ (Stokes) | Opposes motion through fluid |

For JEE Main and Advanced, the primary focus is static and kinetic friction.

## SECTION 2 — Static Friction

---

### 2.1 The Self-Adjusting Nature of Static Friction

Static friction is remarkable: it adjusts its magnitude to exactly balance whatever force tries to cause relative motion, up to a maximum limit.

**Experiment:** Place a block on a rough table. Apply a gradually increasing horizontal force $F$.

| Applied Force $F$ | Static Friction $f_s$ | Motion |
|-------------------|----------------------|--------|
| 0 | 0 | At rest |
| 5 N | 5 N | At rest |
| 10 N | 10 N | At rest |
| 15 N (= $f_{s,max}$) | 15 N | On verge of sliding |
| 16 N | 14 N (kinetic) | Sliding |

This self-adjusting behaviour is a **constraint force** — static friction is whatever value is needed to maintain no relative motion, subject to the constraint $f_s \leq \mu_s N$.

---

### 2.2 Limiting Friction

<Formula label="Limiting (Maximum) Static Friction">
$$f_{s,max} = \mu_s N$$
</Formula>

where $\mu_s$ is the **coefficient of static friction** (dimensionless) and $N$ is the normal force.

**Key facts:**
- $\mu_s$ depends only on the pair of materials in contact and surface condition, not on area or speed
- Typical values: rubber on concrete ≈ 0.8–1.0, steel on steel ≈ 0.7–0.8, ice on ice ≈ 0.03–0.05
- When block is on the verge of motion, $f_s = \mu_s N$ (equality, not inequality)

<Callout type="warning">
**The Most Misunderstood Fact in Friction**

Static friction is NOT always $\mu_s N$. That is only its **maximum** value. Actual static friction can be anything from 0 to $\mu_s N$.

Example: A 10 kg block on a surface with $\mu_s = 0.5$. If no horizontal force is applied, friction = **0** (not $0.5 \times 10 \times 10 = 50$ N). Friction adjusts to oppose whatever force is trying to cause motion.
</Callout>

---

### 2.3 Coefficient of Static Friction — Empirical Laws

The **Amontons–Coulomb Laws of Friction** (empirical, not derived from first principles):

1. Friction force is proportional to normal force: $f_s \leq \mu_s N$
2. Friction is independent of the apparent contact area
3. Kinetic friction is independent of sliding speed (approximately)

These laws are remarkably accurate for dry surfaces over a wide range of conditions. They break down at very high pressures, very high speeds, or at the nanoscale.

## SECTION 3 — Kinetic Friction

---

### 3.1 Definition and Formula

Once surfaces begin sliding, the friction force drops to a lower, nearly constant value:

<Formula label="Kinetic Friction">
$$f_k = \mu_k N$$
</Formula>

where $\mu_k$ is the **coefficient of kinetic friction**.

**Always: $\mu_k < \mu_s$**

This is why:
- It takes more force to start a heavy box sliding than to keep it sliding
- Anti-lock braking systems (ABS) keep tires on the verge of sliding (static friction) rather than fully sliding (kinetic friction), giving more braking force

---

### 3.2 Properties of Kinetic Friction

- **Constant magnitude** (independent of speed, for moderate speeds)
- **Independent of contact area**
- **Opposes relative motion** (always acts opposite to the direction of sliding)
- **Smaller than maximum static friction**: $f_k = \mu_k N < \mu_s N$

## SECTION 4 — Direction of Friction

---

### 4.1 The Cardinal Rule

<Callout type="important">
**Friction opposes the RELATIVE MOTION (or tendency of relative motion) between the two surfaces in contact — not the absolute motion of either body.**
</Callout>

This distinction matters enormously. The relative motion is the motion of the surface of one body as seen from the other body.

---

### 4.2 Finding Friction Direction — Systematic Method

<Step n={1}>
**Identify the two surfaces in contact** (e.g., block bottom and table top).
</Step>

<Step n={2}>
**Determine the tendency of relative motion** of one surface with respect to the other. Ask: "If friction were absent, which way would the block slide on the table?"
</Step>

<Step n={3}>
**Friction opposes that tendency.** It acts opposite to the direction in which the block would slide without friction.
</Step>

---

### 4.3 Common Cases Where Direction Is Non-Obvious

**Case 1: Block on accelerating truck**

A block sits on a truck that accelerates forward. If the block has insufficient friction, it would slide backward relative to the truck. Therefore, friction on the block acts **forward** (in the direction of truck's acceleration).

**Case 2: Block dragged by a rope on a rough surface**

The block tends to slide forward relative to the ground. Friction on block acts **backward**.

**Case 3: A person walking**

When you push your foot backward against the ground, the ground pushes you forward. Friction on your foot from the ground acts **forward** (this is what propels you).

**Case 4: Block on a rotating turntable**

Block tends to slide outward (due to its inertia in the rotating frame). Static friction acts **inward** (toward center) to keep the block rotating with the turntable.

## SECTION 5 — Angle of Friction and Angle of Repose

---

### 5.1 Angle of Friction

When friction is at its maximum value, the resultant of normal reaction $N$ and friction $f_{max}$ makes an angle $\lambda$ with the normal:

$$\tan\lambda = \frac{f_{max}}{N} = \frac{\mu_s N}{N} = \mu_s$$

<Formula label="Angle of Friction">
$$\lambda = \arctan(\mu_s)$$
</Formula>

The **cone of friction** (or friction cone) is a cone of half-angle $\lambda$ around the normal direction. If the resultant applied force lies within this cone, no sliding occurs. If it lies outside, sliding occurs.

### 5.2 Angle of Repose

The **angle of repose** $\theta_r$ is the maximum angle of an inclined surface at which an object placed on it will remain stationary without sliding.

**Derivation:**

On an incline of angle $\theta$:
- Component of gravity along incline: $mg\sin\theta$ (down)
- Normal force: $N = mg\cos\theta$
- Maximum static friction: $\mu_s mg\cos\theta$ (up the incline)

For equilibrium: $mg\sin\theta \leq \mu_s mg\cos\theta$

$$\tan\theta \leq \mu_s$$

At the critical angle (angle of repose):

<Formula label="Angle of Repose">
$$\tan\theta_r = \mu_s \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad \theta_r = \lambda \text{ (angle of friction)}$$
</Formula>

**The angle of repose equals the angle of friction.** This is a beautiful and exact relationship.

<Callout type="tip">
**Practical Use**

If you see a problem stating "block just begins to slide at angle $\theta$," then $\mu_s = \tan\theta$.

Conversely, to find whether a block will slide: compare $\tan\theta$ with $\mu_s$.
- $\tan\theta < \mu_s$: Block stays (static friction < limiting value)
- $\tan\theta = \mu_s$: Block on verge of sliding
- $\tan\theta > \mu_s$: Block slides (kinetic friction takes over)
</Callout>

**Forces on block (axes: along incline, perpendicular to incline):**

Perpendicular to incline (no acceleration):
$$N = mg\cos\theta$$

Along incline (positive = down, block slides down so $f$ acts up):
$$ma = mg\sin\theta - f_k = mg\sin\theta - \mu_k N = mg\sin\theta - \mu_k mg\cos\theta$$

<Formula label="Acceleration Down a Rough Incline">
$$a = g(\sin\theta - \mu_k\cos\theta)$$
</Formula>

**Condition for sliding:** $a > 0 \Rightarrow \sin\theta > \mu_k\cos\theta \Rightarrow \tan\theta > \mu_k$

Since $\mu_k < \mu_s$, the condition for sliding is more easily met with kinetic friction ($\mu_k$) than the condition for starting to slide ($\mu_s$). Once sliding, $\mu_k$ is what matters.

---

### 6.2 Block Pushed Up a Rough Incline

When a block is pushed up, both gravity component ($mg\sin\theta$) and kinetic friction ($\mu_k mg\cos\theta$) act **down** the incline.

Deceleration going up:
$$a_{up} = g(\sin\theta + \mu_k\cos\theta) \quad \text{(magnitude)}$$

**Important:** $a_{up} > a_{down}$ — a block decelerates faster going up than it accelerates going down.

---

### 6.3 Block in Equilibrium on Rough Incline

For a block to remain stationary on an incline, static friction adjusts to balance:

$$f_s = mg\sin\theta \quad \text{(static friction, up the incline)}$$

This is valid as long as:
$$mg\sin\theta \leq \mu_s mg\cos\theta \Rightarrow \tan\theta \leq \mu_s$$

If an additional force $F$ is applied, friction adjusts accordingly.

---

### 6.4 Range of Applied Force for Equilibrium on Rough Incline

A block of mass $m$ on a rough incline (angle $\theta > \theta_r$). What range of force $P$ (applied along the incline) keeps the block in equilibrium?

**Case A: Block tends to slide down** (friction acts up):

$$P + \mu_s mg\cos\theta \geq mg\sin\theta$$
$$P \geq mg(\sin\theta - \mu_s\cos\theta) = mg\tan(\theta - \lambda)\cos\theta$$

**Case B: Block tends to slide up** (friction acts down):

$$P \leq mg\sin\theta + \mu_s mg\cos\theta$$
$$P \leq mg(\sin\theta + \mu_s\cos\theta) = mg\tan(\theta + \lambda)\cos\theta$$

<Formula label="Range of Force for Equilibrium on Incline">
$$mg(\sin\theta - \mu_s\cos\theta) \leq P \leq mg(\sin\theta + \mu_s\cos\theta)$$
</Formula>

**Step 1: Find normal force.**

Resolve forces perpendicular to incline:
$$N = mg\cos\theta + P\sin\theta$$

Note: $P$ has a component $P\sin\theta$ pressing the block into the incline, increasing $N$.

**Step 2: Check tendency of motion** (assume block tends to slide up when $P$ is large):

Along incline (up positive):
$$P\cos\theta - mg\sin\theta - \mu_s N = 0 \quad \text{(just about to slide up)}$$

Substituting $N$:
$$P\cos\theta - mg\sin\theta - \mu_s(mg\cos\theta + P\sin\theta) = 0$$

$$P(\cos\theta - \mu_s\sin\theta) = mg(\sin\theta + \mu_s\cos\theta)$$

<Formula label="Horizontal Force to Just Slide Block UP Incline">
$$P = mg \cdot \frac{\sin\theta + \mu_s\cos\theta}{\cos\theta - \mu_s\sin\theta} = mg\tan(\theta + \lambda)$$
</Formula>

where $\lambda = \arctan\mu_s$ is the angle of friction.

---

### 7.2 Minimum Force to Move a Block on a Horizontal Surface

A block (mass $m$) on a rough horizontal surface ($\mu_s$). Force $F$ applied at angle $\alpha$ above horizontal. Find $\alpha$ that minimises $F$ to just start motion.

**Normal force:** $N = mg - F\sin\alpha$

**Along horizontal (about to slide):** $F\cos\alpha = \mu_s N = \mu_s(mg - F\sin\alpha)$

$$F(\cos\alpha + \mu_s\sin\alpha) = \mu_s mg$$

$$F = \frac{\mu_s mg}{\cos\alpha + \mu_s\sin\alpha}$$

To minimise $F$, maximise the denominator $D = \cos\alpha + \mu_s\sin\alpha$:

$$\frac{dD}{d\alpha} = -\sin\alpha + \mu_s\cos\alpha = 0 \Rightarrow \tan\alpha = \mu_s \Rightarrow \alpha = \lambda$$

<Formula label="Minimum Force to Move Block on Flat Surface">
$$F_{min} = \frac{\mu_s mg}{\sqrt{1 + \mu_s^2}} = mg\sin\lambda$$

Applied at angle $\alpha = \lambda$ (angle of friction) above horizontal.
</Formula>

<Callout type="tip">
**Beautiful Result**

The minimum force to move a block is always applied at angle equal to the angle of friction $\lambda = \arctan\mu_s$. This is true both for sliding on a horizontal surface and for pushing up an incline. The physics: you're most efficiently using your force when the applied force direction is exactly perpendicular to the resultant of $N$ and $f_{max}$.
</Callout>

**Total normal force on floor:** $N_1 = (M + m)g$

**Friction from floor on A:** $f_1 = \mu_1(M+m)g$

**Friction from A on B and B on A:** $f_2 = \mu_2 mg$ (maximum if B slides on A)

**Case 1: A and B move together** (no sliding between them):

$$a = \frac{F - f_1}{M+m} = \frac{F - \mu_1(M+m)g}{M+m}$$

For B to move with A, friction must provide B's acceleration. In B's free body diagram only friction $f_2$ acts horizontally:

$$f_2 = ma = \frac{m\big(F - \mu_1(M+m)g\big)}{M+m}$$

For no slipping between A and B, we need $f_2 \leq \mu_2 mg$:

$$\frac{m\big(F - \mu_1(M+m)g\big)}{M+m} \leq \mu_2 mg \quad\Rightarrow\quad F \leq (\mu_1 + \mu_2)(M+m)g$$

**Maximum $F$ for no slipping:**

<Formula label="Max Force for No Slipping (Block on Block)">
$$F_{max} = \mu_1(M+m)g + \mu_2(M+m)g = (M+m)(\mu_1 + \mu_2)g$$
</Formula>

**Case 2: A slides under B** (F exceeds $F_{max}$):

- Friction on B from A: $f_2 = \mu_2 mg$ (kinetic, forward on B)
- Friction on A from floor: $f_1 = \mu_1(M+m)g$ (backward on A)
- Friction on A from B (reaction): $\mu_2 mg$ (backward on A)

$$a_B = \frac{\mu_2 mg}{m} = \mu_2 g$$

$$a_A = \frac{F - \mu_1(M+m)g - \mu_2 mg}{M}$$

---

### 8.2 Force on Upper Block

Same setup but force $F$ applied to B (upper block).

**Maximum $F$ for no slipping between A and B.** For the blocks to move together, A must accelerate along with B. Treating both as one system (floor friction $f_1 = \mu_1(M+m)g$ opposes motion):

$$a = \frac{F - \mu_1(M+m)g}{M+m}$$

The only horizontal forces on the lower block A are the friction $f_2$ from B (forward, since B is driven forward and drags A) and the floor friction (backward):

$$Ma = f_2 - \mu_1(M+m)g$$

Solving for $f_2$ and substituting $a$:

$$f_2 = Ma + \mu_1(M+m)g = \frac{MF + \mu_1 m(M+m)g}{M+m}$$

For no slipping we need $f_2 \leq \mu_2 mg$:

$$\frac{MF + \mu_1 m(M+m)g}{M+m} \leq \mu_2 mg \quad\Rightarrow\quad F \leq \frac{(M+m)mg(\mu_2 - \mu_1)}{M}$$

This requires $\mu_2 > \mu_1$; otherwise no force on the upper block can keep the two moving together against the floor friction.

Horizontal: $f = N_W$

Torque about base (anti-clockwise positive):

$$N_W \cdot L\sin\theta - mg \cdot \frac{L}{2}\cos\theta = 0$$

Since $f = N_W$:

$$f = \frac{mg}{2\tan\theta}$$

$$\frac{mg}{2\tan\theta} \leq \mu_s mg \Rightarrow \tan\theta \geq \frac{1}{2\mu_s}$$

$$\tan\theta_{min} = \frac{1}{2\mu_s} \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad \theta_{min} = \arctan\left(\frac{1}{2\mu_s}\right)$$

Friction condition: $f = N_W \leq \mu_s(m+M)g$

## SECTION 10 — Rolling Friction

---

### 10.1 What Is Rolling Friction?

When a wheel rolls on a surface, the contact zone undergoes repeated compression and relaxation (elastic deformation). The leading edge of the contact zone presses down, while the trailing edge lifts. This creates a net resistive torque opposing rolling.

**Rolling friction force:**
$$f_r = \mu_r N$$

where $\mu_r \ll \mu_k$. Typical values: $\mu_r \approx 0.001$–$0.005$ for steel on steel (rail transport), $\mu_r \approx 0.01$–$0.03$ for rubber on concrete.

**Why wheels are better than sliders:** Rolling friction is typically 100–1000 times smaller than sliding friction. This is the fundamental reason wheels revolutionised transportation.

---

### 10.2 Conditions for Pure Rolling (No Slipping)

For a wheel of radius $R$ rolling without slipping:

$$v_{centre} = R\omega$$

The contact point is instantaneously at rest. Static friction acts at the contact point but does no work (contact point has zero velocity).

**Friction in rolling without slipping is static friction**, not kinetic.

---

## SECTION 11 — Belt and Pulley Friction

---

### 11.1 Belt Friction (Capstan Equation)

When a rope or belt wraps around a cylinder (like a rope over a rough capstan), the tension increases exponentially with the angle of wrap:

$$T_2 = T_1 e^{\mu\theta}$$

where $T_1$ = smaller tension, $T_2$ = larger tension, $\theta$ = total angle of wrap (radians), $\mu$ = coefficient of friction.

This is the **capstan equation** — it explains why a small force can hold a large load when a rope is wrapped around a post multiple times.

<Callout type="info">
**Capstan Effect in Practice**

A rope wrapped $n$ times around a post (angle of wrap $= 2\pi n$ radians) with $\mu = 0.3$:

$T_2/T_1 = e^{0.3 \times 2\pi} = e^{1.885} \approx 6.6$ per wrap.

With 3 wraps: $T_2/T_1 = e^{0.3 \times 6\pi} \approx 286$.

One person holding 1 N can resist 286 N force! This is why sailors could control heavy ship sails.
</Callout>

## SECTION 12 — Advanced Applications

---

### 12.1 Friction and Circular Motion — Vehicle on Curved Road

**Flat circular road:** A car of mass $m$ moving at speed $v$ on a flat road of radius $R$.

Centripetal force provided by static friction:

$$\frac{mv^2}{R} \leq \mu_s mg$$

$$v_{max} = \sqrt{\mu_s gR}$$

**Banked road:** Road banked at angle $\theta$. Even without friction, a component of normal force provides centripetal force:

$$N\sin\theta = \frac{mv^2}{R} \qquad N\cos\theta = mg$$

$$v_{ideal} = \sqrt{Rg\tan\theta}$$

(At this speed, no friction is needed.) For higher or lower speeds, friction helps.

---

### 12.2 Friction in Pulley Problems

When a string passes over a **rough pulley** (or a rough peg), the belt friction equation applies:

$$\frac{T_2}{T_1} = e^{\mu\alpha}$$

where $\alpha$ is the angle of contact. For a half-turn ($\alpha = \pi$):

$$\frac{T_2}{T_1} = e^{\mu\pi}$$

For JEE problems involving a string over a rough nail or peg, use this ratio.

---

### 12.3 Two Blocks Connected Over a Rough Incline

Block A on rough incline (angle $\theta$, $\mu$), string over smooth pulley at top, block B hanging vertically.

**For system to move (A up, B down):**
$$m_B g - T = m_B a$$
$$T - m_A g\sin\theta - \mu m_A g\cos\theta = m_A a$$

Adding: $a = \frac{(m_B - m_A\sin\theta - \mu m_A\cos\theta)g}{m_A + m_B}$

**Range of $m_B$ for equilibrium:**

$$m_A(\sin\theta - \mu\cos\theta) \leq m_B \leq m_A(\sin\theta + \mu\cos\theta)$$

## SECTION 13 — Impending Motion and Friction Cones

---

### 13.1 Three States of Friction

For any contact surface, friction is in one of three states:

| State | Condition | Friction magnitude | Direction |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|-----------|
| No tendency to move | No external force component along surface | 0 | — |
| Static (below limit) | $F_{applied} < f_{s,max}$ | $= F_{applied}$ | Opposes tendency |
| Limiting | $F_{applied} = f_{s,max}$ | $= \mu_s N$ | Opposes tendency |
| Kinetic | $F_{applied} > f_{s,max}$ | $= \mu_k N$ | Opposes relative motion |

---

### 13.2 Self-Locking

A screw or wedge is "self-locking" when the angle of the thread/wedge is less than the angle of friction. In such cases, no force is needed to maintain the position — friction alone prevents reversal.

**Condition for self-locking:** $\alpha < \lambda$ (where $\alpha$ = lead angle, $\lambda$ = angle of friction)

This is why screws don't unscrew themselves and wedges stay put.

## SECTION 14 — Error Analysis

---

| # | Mistake | Correct Approach |
|---|---------|-----------------|
| 1 | Assuming $f = \mu_s N$ always | Static friction is $f \leq \mu_s N$; equals $\mu_s N$ only at limiting case |
| 2 | Using $\mu_k$ instead of $\mu_s$ for stationary bodies | Use $\mu_k$ only when body is actually sliding |
| 3 | Forgetting that $\mu_k < \mu_s$ | The transition from static to kinetic involves a drop in friction |
| 4 | Wrong direction of friction on one of two connected bodies | On block A, friction from B is reaction to friction on B from A — opposite directions |
| 5 | Friction $= \mu N$ on incline but using $N = mg$ | On incline: $N = mg\cos\theta$, so $f_k = \mu mg\cos\theta$ |
| 6 | Forgetting friction changes sign when motion reverses | A block going up has both gravity and friction opposing it; coming down, only gravity opposes, friction now opposes downward motion |
| 7 | In ladder problems, forgetting torque equation | Three equations needed: $\sum F_x = 0$, $\sum F_y = 0$, $\sum \tau = 0$ |
| 8 | Taking angle of repose = $\arctan(\mu_k)$ | Angle of repose = $\arctan(\mu_s)$ (static friction, not kinetic) |
| 9 | Applying belt friction formula for smooth pulleys | $T_2 = T_1 e^{\mu\theta}$ only for rough pulley/peg |
| 10 | Forgetting normal force increases when force has component into surface | $N = mg + F\sin\alpha$ when force has component pressing block into surface |

## SECTION 16 — Solved Examples

---

### JEE Main Level Examples

**M1.** A 5 kg block on a rough surface ($\mu_s = 0.5$, $\mu_k = 0.4$, $g = 10$ m/s²). Applied force = 20 N. Find friction and acceleration.

**Solution:**
$f_{s,max} = 0.5 \times 5 \times 10 = 25$ N. Since $20 < 25$: block stays. $f_s = 20$ N, $a = 0$.

---

**M2.** Same block, applied force = 30 N.

**Solution:**
$30 > 25$ N → slides. $f_k = 0.4 \times 50 = 20$ N.
$a = (30 - 20)/5 = \mathbf{2 \text{ m/s}^2}$

---

**M3.** A block of 4 kg on a rough 37° incline ($\mu_k = 0.3$). Find acceleration as it slides down. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
$N = 4 \times 10 \times \cos 37° = 32$ N. $f_k = 0.3 \times 32 = 9.6$ N.
$mg\sin 37° = 24$ N.
$a = (24 - 9.6)/4 = \mathbf{3.6 \text{ m/s}^2}$

---

**M4.** What is the minimum coefficient of static friction needed for a block to remain on a 30° incline without sliding?

**Solution:**
$\mu_s \geq \tan 30° = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \approx \mathbf{0.577}$

---

**M5.** A block of 3 kg is pushed along a horizontal rough surface ($\mu_k = 0.25$) by a force 15 N applied at 37° below horizontal. Find acceleration. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
$F_y = -15\sin 37° = -9$ N (pressing down). $N = 3 \times 10 + 9 = 39$ N.
$f_k = 0.25 \times 39 = 9.75$ N.
$a = (15\cos 37° - 9.75)/3 = (12 - 9.75)/3 = \mathbf{0.75 \text{ m/s}^2}$

---

**M6.** A block on a rough incline (angle 45°, $\mu_s = 0.6$). Is it in equilibrium? Find friction.

**Solution:**
A block stays in equilibrium on an incline only if $\tan\theta \leq \mu_s$. Here $\tan 45° = 1 > \mu_s = 0.6$, so the block **cannot** be in equilibrium — it slides down.

Equivalently, the angle of repose is $\theta_r = \arctan\mu_s = \arctan 0.6 \approx 31°$; since $45° > 31°$, sliding is expected. The maximum friction available, $\mu_s mg\cos45° \approx 0.42\,mg$, is less than the driving component $mg\sin45° \approx 0.71\,mg$.

---

**M7.** A 10 kg ladder (length 5 m) leans against a smooth wall at 60° to the floor. $\mu_s = 0.4$ between ladder and floor. Find friction and whether ladder slips. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
$N_W = \dfrac{mg}{2\tan\theta} = \dfrac{100}{2\tan 60°} = \dfrac{100}{2\sqrt{3}} = \dfrac{50}{\sqrt{3}} \approx 28.9$ N

$f = N_W \approx 28.9$ N. $N_F = mg = 100$ N.

$\mu_s N_F = 0.4 \times 100 = 40$ N $> 28.9$ N → **Ladder does not slip**. ✓

---

**M8.** Find the minimum angle for the same ladder not to slip. ($\mu_s = 0.4$)

**Solution:**
$\tan\theta_{min} = \dfrac{1}{2\mu_s} = \dfrac{1}{0.8} = 1.25$

$\theta_{min} = \arctan(1.25) \approx \mathbf{51.3°}$

---

**M9.** A block (5 kg) is on a rough horizontal surface. A force is applied at what angle (above horizontal) gives minimum required force to start motion? $\mu_s = 0.4$, $g = 10$ m/s².

**Solution:**
Optimal angle = angle of friction = $\arctan(0.4) \approx 21.8°$

$F_{min} = \dfrac{\mu_s mg}{\sqrt{1+\mu_s^2}} = \dfrac{0.4 \times 50}{\sqrt{1.16}} = \dfrac{20}{1.077} \approx \mathbf{18.6 \text{ N}}$

---

**M10.** Block A (6 kg) on rough floor ($\mu_k = 0.3$). Block B (2 kg) sits on top of A. $\mu_s = 0.4$ between A and B. Force 30 N is applied to A. Do they move together? ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
$f_1 = 0.3 \times (6+2) \times 10 = 24$ N (floor on A).

If together: $a = (30 - 24)/8 = 0.75$ m/s².

Required friction on B: $f_2 = 2 \times 0.75 = 1.5$ N.

$f_{2,max} = 0.4 \times 2 \times 10 = 8$ N $\gg 1.5$ N → **They move together**. $f_2 = 1.5$ N.

---

**M11.** Same system but force = 80 N. Find accelerations.

**Solution:**
If together: $a = (80-24)/8 = 7$ m/s². Required friction on B: $2 \times 7 = 14$ N.

$f_{2,max} = 8$ N $< 14$ N → **They slide**.

$a_B = f_{2}/m_B = 8/2 = 4$ m/s² (friction from A on B, forward, kinetic now)

$a_A = (80 - 24 - 8)/6 = 48/6 = \mathbf{8 \text{ m/s}^2}$

---

**M12.** A chain (mass 5 kg, length 2 m) lies on a rough table ($\mu_s = 0.3$). Find max hanging length. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
$f_{max} = \dfrac{\mu_s}{1+\mu_s} = \dfrac{0.3}{1.3} = 0.231$

Maximum hanging length $= 0.231 \times 2 \approx \mathbf{0.46 \text{ m}}$

---

**M13.** A block is projected **up** a rough incline (37°, $\mu_k = 0.2$) with initial speed 5 m/s. Find the distance it travels before stopping. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
Going up, both gravity and friction act down the slope, so the deceleration is:

$$a = g(\sin 37° + \mu_k\cos 37°) = 10(0.6 + 0.2 \times 0.8) = 7.6 \text{ m/s}^2$$

$v^2 = u^2 - 2as \Rightarrow 0 = 25 - 2 \times 7.6 \times s$

$s = 25/15.2 \approx \mathbf{1.64 \text{ m}}$

---

**M14.** A block of mass 2 kg is placed on a block of mass 8 kg. The lower block is on a smooth floor. $\mu = 0.3$ between blocks. A force 20 N is applied to the lower block. Find accelerations. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
Floor is smooth. Friction only between blocks.

If together: $a = 20/10 = 2$ m/s². Friction needed on upper block: $2 \times 2 = 4$ N.

$f_{max} = 0.3 \times 2 \times 10 = 6$ N $> 4$ N → **Move together**. $a = \mathbf{2 \text{ m/s}^2}$.

---

**M15.** Find max force on upper block (same system) before slipping starts.

**Solution:**
Force $F$ on lower block. Together: $a = F/10$.

Friction on upper block: $2F/10 = F/5 \leq 6$ N.

$F_{max} = 30$ N. At this force, $a = 3$ m/s².

---

### JEE Advanced Level Examples

**A1.** Block A (3 kg) on rough incline (37°, $\mu_s = 0.5$, $\mu_k = 0.4$). Block B (2 kg) hangs vertically, connected by string. Find range of B's mass for equilibrium. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
Forces on A (along incline):
- $mg_A\sin 37° = 18$ N (down incline)
- $f_{max} = \mu_s \times 3 \times 10 \times \cos 37° = 0.5 \times 24 = 12$ N

**Case 1: B too heavy — system moves, A goes up:**
$T = m_B g$; for A: $T - 18 - 12 = 0 \Rightarrow T = 30$ N $\Rightarrow m_B = 3$ kg.

**Case 2: B too light — system moves, A goes down:**
$T - m_B g = 0$; for A: $18 - 12 - T = 0 \Rightarrow T = 6$ N $\Rightarrow m_B = 0.6$ kg.

**Equilibrium range: $0.6 \text{ kg} \leq m_B \leq 3 \text{ kg}$**

**A2.** A horizontal force $P$ is applied to a block (3 kg) on a rough incline (53°, $\mu_s = 0.4$). Find (a) $P$ to just move block up, (b) $P$ to just prevent block from sliding down. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
$\lambda = \arctan 0.4 \approx 21.8°$

**(a) Just moving up:** $P = mg\tan(\theta + \lambda) = 30\tan(53° + 21.8°) = 30\tan(74.8°) = 30 \times 3.73 \approx \mathbf{112 \text{ N}}$

**(b) Just preventing slide down:** $P = mg\tan(\theta - \lambda) = 30\tan(53° - 21.8°) = 30\tan(31.2°) = 30 \times 0.606 \approx \mathbf{18.2 \text{ N}}$

**A3.** Three blocks A, B, C stacked vertically (C on B on A on rough floor). Masses: $m_A = 4$ kg, $m_B = 3$ kg, $m_C = 2$ kg. $\mu$ between all surfaces = 0.3. Force 80 N on A. Do all blocks move together? If not, find accelerations. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
Total mass = 9 kg. Floor friction: $f_1 = 0.3 \times 9 \times 10 = 27$ N.

If together: $a = (80 - 27)/9 = 53/9 \approx 5.89$ m/s²

Friction needed to accelerate C: $f_{C} = 2 \times 5.89 = 11.78$ N.
$f_{max,BC} = 0.3 \times 2 \times 10 = 6$ N $< 11.78$ N → C **slips** on B.

Now check B+C as system on A:
Friction needed to accelerate B+C: $5 \times 5.89 = 29.4$ N.
$f_{max,AB} = 0.3 \times 5 \times 10 = 15$ N $< 29.4$ N → B **slips** on A too.

All three blocks slip on each other.

$a_C = f_{BC}/m_C$: C receives kinetic friction from B: $f_{k,BC} = 0.3 \times 2 \times 10 = 6$ N.
$a_C = 6/2 = 3$ m/s²

$a_B$: Friction from C on B = 6 N (backward). Friction from A on B = $f_{k,AB} = 0.3 \times 5 \times 10 = 15$ N (forward).
$a_B = (15 - 6)/3 = 3$ m/s²

Interesting: $a_B = a_C = 3$ m/s². So B and C actually move together (no relative motion)! We must recheck: if B and C move together, we need friction on B from A to be $\leq f_{max,AB} = 15$ N.

If B+C together: net force on B+C from A (friction) = $(m_B + m_C) \times a_{BC}$. From A: $a_A$ needs to be found.

$a_A = (80 - f_{1,floor} - f_{AB,kinetic})/m_A$

$f_{floor} = 0.3 \times 9 \times 10 = 27$ N. $f_{AB} = 15$ N (B+C slip on A).

$a_A = (80 - 27 - 15)/4 = 38/4 = 9.5$ m/s²

Friction on B+C from A: 15 N → $a_{BC} = 15/5 = 3$ m/s².

Verify B, C don't slip: needed friction between B and C to give C acceleration 3 m/s² = $2 \times 3 = 6$ N ≤ $f_{max,BC} = 6$ N. Exactly at limit!

**Accelerations:** $a_A = 9.5$ m/s², $a_{B+C} = 3$ m/s². B and C move together.

**A4.** A rope of mass $m$ and length $L$ is placed on a rough table with coefficient $\mu$. The rope hangs over the edge. Initially a length $\ell_0$ hangs. The system starts from rest. Find velocity when length $\ell$ hangs. (Assume $\ell_0$ is greater than the minimum hanging length for motion.)

**Solution:**
Let $x$ = hanging length at any time. On-table length = $L - x$.

Weight of hanging part: $\dfrac{mx}{L} g$

Friction on table part (kinetic, opposing motion): $\mu \cdot \dfrac{m(L-x)}{L} g$

Net force: $F_{net} = \dfrac{mg}{L}[x - \mu(L-x)] = \dfrac{mg}{L}[x(1+\mu) - \mu L]$

By Newton's 2nd Law: $m \ddot{x} = \dfrac{mg}{L}[x(1+\mu) - \mu L]$

Using $v\,dv/dx = \ddot{x}$:

$v\,\dfrac{dv}{dx} = \dfrac{g}{L}[(1+\mu)x - \mu L]$

$v^2 = \dfrac{2g}{L}\int_{\ell_0}^{\ell} [(1+\mu)x - \mu L]\,dx$

$= \dfrac{2g}{L}\left[\frac{(1+\mu)}{2}(x^2) - \mu Lx\right]_{\ell_0}^{\ell}$

$= \dfrac{g}{L}\left[(1+\mu)(\ell^2 - \ell_0^2) - 2\mu L(\ell - \ell_0)\right]$

$$v = \sqrt{\dfrac{g(\ell - \ell_0)[(1+\mu)(\ell+\ell_0) - 2\mu L]}{L}}$$

**A5.** A person stands on a plank on a rough floor. The plank can slide on the floor ($\mu_1$). The person can slide on the plank ($\mu_2$). The person pushes horizontally against a wall. Find conditions under which the person stays still relative to the plank.

**Solution:**
Let force person exerts on wall = $F$. By Newton's 3rd, wall pushes person with $F$ (horizontal, away from wall).

For person: $F - f_{person-plank} = m_{person} \cdot a_{person}$

For plank: $f_{person-plank} - f_{floor-plank} = m_{plank} \cdot a_{plank}$

For person to stay still relative to plank: $a_{person} = a_{plank}$.

Let $a$ = common acceleration.

$(m_p + m_P) a = F - f_{floor} = F - \mu_1(m_p + m_P)g$ (if plank slides on floor)

Friction between person and plank:
$m_p a = F - f_{person-plank}$ → $f_{person-plank} = F - m_p a = F - \dfrac{m_p(F - \mu_1(m_p+m_P)g)}{m_p+m_P}$

$= \dfrac{Fm_P + m_p\mu_1(m_p+m_P)g}{m_p+m_P} = \dfrac{m_P F}{m_p+m_P} + m_p\mu_1 g$

For no slipping: $f_{person-plank} \leq \mu_2 m_p g$:

$\dfrac{m_P F}{m_p+m_P} + m_p\mu_1 g \leq \mu_2 m_p g$

$F \leq \dfrac{(m_p+m_P)m_p(\mu_2 - \mu_1)g}{m_P}$

(requires $\mu_2 > \mu_1$ for any valid force range)

### Formula Sheet

<Formula label="Friction — Complete Formula Sheet">

**Static friction:** $0 \leq f_s \leq \mu_s N$

**Limiting friction:** $f_{s,max} = \mu_s N$

**Kinetic friction:** $f_k = \mu_k N$, always $\mu_k < \mu_s$

**Angle of friction:** $\tan\lambda = \mu_s$

**Angle of repose:** $\tan\theta_r = \mu_s$ (equals angle of friction)

**On smooth incline:** $a = g\sin\theta$

**Sliding DOWN rough incline:** $a = g(\sin\theta - \mu_k\cos\theta)$

**Sliding UP rough incline:** $a = g(\sin\theta + \mu_k\cos\theta)$ (deceleration)

**Equilibrium on rough incline:** $\tan\theta \leq \mu_s$

**Horizontal force to push block up incline:** $P = mg\tan(\theta + \lambda)$

**Minimum force on flat surface:** $F_{min} = mg\sin\lambda$, at angle $\alpha = \lambda$ above horizontal

**Max fraction of chain hanging:** $f_{max} = \dfrac{\mu_s}{1+\mu_s}$

**Ladder (smooth wall):** $N_W = \dfrac{mg}{2\tan\theta}$; min angle: $\tan\theta_{min} = \dfrac{1}{2\mu_s}$

**Belt/rope friction (capstan):** $T_2 = T_1 e^{\mu\alpha}$

**Range of $P$ (along incline) for equilibrium:**
$mg(\sin\theta - \mu_s\cos\theta) \leq P \leq mg(\sin\theta + \mu_s\cos\theta)$

</Formula>

### Last-Minute Tips

<Callout type="tip">
**5 Golden Rules for Friction Problems**

1. **Static friction is self-adjusting** — always calculate $f_{s,max}$ first, then compare with the applied force component to decide if the body moves.

2. **Check the direction carefully** — friction opposes *relative motion* between surfaces, not the absolute motion of the body. Draw both surfaces and ask "which way would one surface slide on the other?"

3. **Always recalculate $N$** — on inclined planes, with angled forces, or in elevators, $N \neq mg$.

4. **For equilibrium range problems** — solve two separate cases: (a) object about to slide in one direction, (b) about to slide the other way. The equilibrium range lies between these two limits.

5. **For block-on-block** — draw separate FBDs and remember friction is an internal force for the combined system but an external force for each individual block.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
**3 Traps That Kill Marks**

1. **Angle of repose trap:** $\tan\theta_r = \mu_s$ (not $\mu_k$!). A block placed on an incline starts sliding based on static friction, not kinetic.

2. **Friction can be zero even on rough surfaces** — if no force tries to cause relative motion, friction is zero. "Rough surface" doesn't mean there's always friction.

3. **In block-on-block problems** — when the lower block has a force applied, the friction on the UPPER block is forward (it's being dragged along). Don't draw it backward just because "friction opposes motion."
</Callout>
