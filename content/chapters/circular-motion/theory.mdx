## SECTION 1 — The Big Picture

---

### 1.1 What Is Circular Motion?

A stone on a string swung overhead. The Moon orbiting Earth. A car turning a corner. A charged particle spiralling in a magnetic field. An electron in a Bohr orbit. All of these involve **circular motion** — motion along a circular path.

What makes circular motion special is that **the direction of velocity changes continuously**, even if speed stays constant. And from Newton's Second Law, a changing velocity means a net force must be acting.

This net force — always directed toward the centre of the circle — is called the **centripetal force**. Understanding it is the key to mastering this entire chapter.

---

### 1.2 Kinematics vs Dynamics of Circular Motion

| Aspect | Kinematics | Dynamics |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Question | How does the particle move? | What forces cause this motion? |
| Tools | $\omega$, $\alpha$, $v$, $a_c$, $a_t$ | Newton's 2nd Law: $F_{net} = ma$ |
| Key result | $a_c = v^2/r = \omega^2 r$ | Identify what provides $F_c = mv^2/r$ |

## SECTION 2 — Angular Kinematics

---

### 2.1 Angular Quantities

For a particle moving in a circle of radius $r$:

| Quantity | Symbol | Definition | SI Unit |
|----------|--------|-----------|---------|
| Angular displacement | $\theta$ | Arc length / radius = $s/r$ | radian (rad) |
| Angular velocity | $\omega$ | $d\theta/dt$ | rad/s |
| Angular acceleration | $\alpha$ | $d\omega/dt$ | rad/s² |
| Frequency | $f$ | Revolutions per second | Hz |
| Time period | $T$ | Time for one revolution | s |

**Relationships:**

$$\omega = 2\pi f = \frac{2\pi}{T} \qquad v = r\omega \qquad T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega}$$

---

### 2.2 Equations of Circular Kinematics

For **constant angular acceleration** $\alpha$, the rotational analogues of the linear SUVAT equations are:

| Linear | Rotational |
|--------|-----------|
| $v = u + at$ | $\omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t$ |
| $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$ | $\theta = \omega_0 t + \frac{1}{2}\alpha t^2$ |
| $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ | $\omega^2 = \omega_0^2 + 2\alpha\theta$ |
| $s = \frac{u+v}{2}t$ | $\theta = \frac{\omega_0+\omega}{2}t$ |

---

### 2.3 Linear and Angular Quantities

For a particle at radius $r$ from the rotation axis:

$$v = r\omega$$

$$a_t = r\alpha \quad \text{(tangential acceleration)}$$

$$a_c = r\omega^2 = \frac{v^2}{r} \quad \text{(centripetal acceleration)}$$

## SECTION 3 — Centripetal Acceleration

---

### 3.1 Derivation from First Principles

Consider a particle moving in a circle of radius $r$ with constant speed $v$. At time $t$, it is at point P with velocity $\vec{v}_1$. At time $t + \Delta t$, it is at Q with velocity $\vec{v}_2$.

The speed is constant ($|\vec{v}_1| = |\vec{v}_2| = v$) but the direction has changed by angle $\Delta\theta$.

The change in velocity: $|\Delta\vec{v}| = 2v\sin(\Delta\theta/2) \approx v\Delta\theta$ (for small $\Delta\theta$)

The arc length: $\Delta s = r\Delta\theta$, so $\Delta t = r\Delta\theta/v$

Centripetal acceleration magnitude:

$$a_c = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{|\Delta\vec{v}|}{\Delta t} = \frac{v\Delta\theta}{r\Delta\theta/v} = \frac{v^2}{r}$$

<Step n={1}>
At P, velocity is $\vec{v}_1$ (tangent to circle). At Q (angle $\Delta\theta$ later), velocity is $\vec{v}_2$ (tangent at Q).
</Step>

<Step n={2}>
$|\Delta\vec{v}| = 2v\sin(\Delta\theta/2) \approx v\Delta\theta$ for small $\Delta\theta$.
</Step>

<Step n={3}>
Direction of $\Delta\vec{v}$: for small $\Delta\theta$, it bisects the angle between $\vec{v}_1$ and $\vec{v}_2$, pointing toward the centre.
</Step>

<Step n={4}>
$a_c = |\Delta\vec{v}|/\Delta t = v\Delta\theta / (r\Delta\theta/v) = v^2/r$, directed toward centre.
</Step>

<Formula label="Centripetal Acceleration">
$$\vec{a}_c = -\frac{v^2}{r}\hat{r} = -\omega^2 r\,\hat{r}$$

Magnitude: $a_c = \dfrac{v^2}{r} = \omega^2 r = v\omega$

Direction: toward the centre of the circle
</Formula>

---

### 3.2 Physical Meaning

Centripetal acceleration is purely a **change in direction** of velocity — not a change in speed. It is always perpendicular to $\vec{v}$, always pointing toward the centre.

<Callout type="warning">
**Centripetal ≠ Centrifugal**

Centripetal acceleration is real — it is the acceleration of the particle as observed in an inertial frame.

Centrifugal "force" is a pseudo force that appears only in the rotating frame of reference. In the ground frame, **there is no centrifugal force**. A stone in circular motion is NOT being "flung outward" — it is constantly being pulled inward.
</Callout>

## SECTION 4 — Centripetal Force

---

### 4.1 What Provides the Centripetal Force?

Centripetal force is NOT a new type of force. It is the **net inward force** that results from forces already present (gravity, tension, normal reaction, friction, etc.).

<Formula label="Centripetal Force">
$$F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r} = m\omega^2 r$$

This must be provided by real forces (tension, gravity, normal reaction, friction, etc.).
</Formula>

<Callout type="important">
**The Most Critical Insight in This Chapter**

"Centripetal force" is not listed alongside gravity and tension in a free body diagram. Instead, when you draw the FBD and apply Newton's 2nd Law toward the centre:

$$\sum F_{toward\,centre} = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

Whatever combination of real forces adds up to $mv^2/r$ inward is providing the centripetal force.
</Callout>

---

### 4.2 What Provides Centripetal Force in Common Situations

| Situation | What provides $F_c$ |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Stone on string (horizontal circle) | Tension $T$ |
| Satellite orbiting Earth | Gravity $mg'$ |
| Car on flat curved road | Static friction |
| Car on banked road (ideal speed) | Component of normal force |
| Electron in Bohr orbit | Electrostatic attraction |
| Ball in vertical circle (at top) | Tension + Weight (both inward) |
| Ball in vertical circle (at bottom) | Tension − Weight (tension inward, weight outward) |

## SECTION 5 — Uniform Circular Motion

---

### 5.1 Characteristics

In **uniform circular motion (UCM)**, speed is constant.

- Speed: $v = $ constant
- Angular velocity: $\omega = $ constant
- Tangential acceleration: $a_t = 0$
- Centripetal acceleration: $a_c = v^2/r$ (constant magnitude, varying direction)
- Net force: purely centripetal, magnitude $mv^2/r$
- Kinetic energy: constant (since speed is constant)
- Work done by centripetal force: **zero** (force $\perp$ displacement at every instant)

## SECTION 6 — Non-Uniform Circular Motion

---

### 6.1 Tangential and Centripetal Acceleration

When speed changes as the particle moves in a circle, there is a **tangential acceleration** $a_t$ in addition to centripetal acceleration $a_c$.

$$a_t = \frac{dv}{dt} = r\alpha$$

$$a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = r\omega^2$$

**Total acceleration:**

$$\vec{a} = \vec{a}_c + \vec{a}_t$$

$$|\vec{a}| = \sqrt{a_c^2 + a_t^2} = \sqrt{\left(\frac{v^2}{r}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dv}{dt}\right)^2}$$

The angle $\phi$ that $\vec{a}$ makes with the radius:

$$\tan\phi = \frac{a_t}{a_c} = \frac{r\alpha}{r\omega^2} = \frac{\alpha}{\omega^2}$$

---

### 6.2 Work and Energy in Non-Uniform Circular Motion

Only the tangential force $F_t = ma_t$ does work (since centripetal force is perpendicular to motion).

$$\text{Power} = F_t \cdot v = ma_t v$$

$$\Delta KE = \int F_t \, ds = \int ma_t \, v \, dt$$

### 7.2 Applying Newton's Second Law at Each Point

**At the TOP** (both $T$ and $mg$ point toward centre, i.e., downward):

$$T + mg = \frac{mv_{top}^2}{L}$$

$$T = \frac{mv_{top}^2}{L} - mg$$

**At the BOTTOM** ($T$ points toward centre (upward), $mg$ points away (downward)):

$$T - mg = \frac{mv_{bot}^2}{L}$$

$$T = \frac{mv_{bot}^2}{L} + mg$$

**At the SIDE** ($T$ points toward centre (horizontal), $mg$ points downward — perpendicular to radius):

$$T = \frac{mv_{side}^2}{L} \qquad \text{(centripetal equation)}$$

$mg$ only affects tangential acceleration at this point, not tension.

---

### 7.3 Minimum Speed at Top — Condition for Complete Circle

The string can only **pull**, never push. So tension $T \geq 0$ always.

At the top, $T$ is minimum (because weight assists centripetal). For minimum tension = 0:

$$0 + mg = \frac{mv_{top,min}^2}{L} \Rightarrow v_{top,min} = \sqrt{gL}$$

<Formula label="Minimum Speed at Top of Vertical Circle (String)">
$$v_{top,min} = \sqrt{gL}$$

At this speed, tension = 0 (string is barely taut). For complete circle: $v_{top} \geq \sqrt{gL}$
</Formula>

---

### 7.4 Minimum Speed at Bottom

Using energy conservation from bottom to top:

$$\frac{1}{2}mv_{bot}^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv_{top}^2 + mg(2L)$$

$$v_{bot}^2 = v_{top}^2 + 4gL$$

For minimum condition ($v_{top} = \sqrt{gL}$):

<Formula label="Minimum Speed at Bottom for Complete Vertical Circle">
$$v_{bot,min} = \sqrt{5gL}$$
</Formula>

---

### 7.5 Tension at Bottom for Minimum Condition

$$T_{bottom} = \frac{mv_{bot}^2}{L} + mg = \frac{m \cdot 5gL}{L} + mg = 5mg + mg = 6mg$$

At the instant of minimum speed for complete rotation, tension at the bottom is **6 times the weight**. This is why swing chains must be strong.

---

**Height and speed.** The bob rises a height $h = L(1 - \cos\theta)$ above the bottom. By energy conservation from the bottom:
$$v^2 = v_{bot}^2 - 2gL(1 - \cos\theta)$$
</Step>

<Step n={2}>
**Radial component of gravity.** The outward radial direction makes angle $\theta$ with the downward vertical, so the component of gravity along the **inward** radial direction is $-mg\cos\theta$ (gravity points outward in the lower half, inward in the upper half).
</Step>

**Newton's 2nd Law (inward positive).** Tension acts inward; gravity contributes $-mg\cos\theta$ inward:
$$T - mg\cos\theta = \frac{mv^2}{L} \quad\Rightarrow\quad T = \frac{mv^2}{L} + mg\cos\theta$$

$$T = \frac{mv_{bot}^2}{L} + mg(3\cos\theta - 2)$$

### 7.7 Vertical Circle — Rod vs String

| Feature | String (flexible) | Rod (rigid) |
|---------|-----------------|-------------|
| Can push? | No ($T \geq 0$) | Yes (compression possible) |
| Min speed at top | $\sqrt{gL}$ | Can be **zero** |
| Min speed at bottom | $\sqrt{5gL}$ | $\sqrt{4gL}$ (just enough energy) |
| Condition for complete circle | $v_{bot} \geq \sqrt{5gL}$ | $v_{bot} \geq \sqrt{4gL}$ (and $v_{top} \geq 0$) |

**Rod derivation:** At the top, normal force from rod can be inward (tension) or outward (compression). Minimum speed: $v_{top} = 0$.

Energy: $\frac{1}{2}mv_{bot}^2 = \frac{1}{2}m(0)^2 + mg(2L) \Rightarrow v_{bot,min} = \sqrt{4gL}$

---

### 7.8 Critical Situations in Vertical Circle

**Case 1: $v_{bot} < \sqrt{2gL}$**
Ball doesn't reach horizontal (side) level. Oscillates in lower arc like a pendulum.

**Case 2: $\sqrt{2gL} \leq v_{bot} < \sqrt{5gL}$**
Ball goes past horizontal but string goes slack before reaching top. It then follows projectile motion.

The angle $\theta$ at which string goes slack (T = 0):

$$0 = \frac{mv^2}{L} + mg\cos\theta \Rightarrow v^2 = -gL\cos\theta$$

Using energy: $v^2 = v_{bot}^2 - 2gL(1-\cos\theta)$

$$-gL\cos\theta = v_{bot}^2 - 2gL + 2gL\cos\theta$$

$$3gL\cos\theta = v_{bot}^2 - 2gL$$

$$\cos\theta = \frac{v_{bot}^2 - 2gL}{3gL}$$

(Valid only when $\cos\theta > -1$, i.e., $v_{bot}^2 > 0$ always, and $\cos\theta < 1$, i.e., $v_{bot}^2 < 5gL$) ✓

**Case 3: $v_{bot} \geq \sqrt{5gL}$**
Complete vertical circle — string taut throughout.

## SECTION 8 — Vertical Circular Motion — Track (Inside)

---

### 8.1 Ball Inside a Circular Track

When a ball moves inside a circular track (like a loop-the-loop), the normal force from the track can act inward or outward:

**At the TOP** (inside of loop): Normal force $N$ acts **downward** (toward centre). Both $N$ and $mg$ are centripetal.

$$N + mg = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

Condition for contact ($N \geq 0$): $v_{top} \geq \sqrt{gr}$

**At the BOTTOM** (inside of loop): Normal force $N$ acts **upward** (toward centre).

$$N - mg = \frac{mv^2}{r} \Rightarrow N = \frac{mv^2}{r} + mg$$

**Minimum speed at top:** $v_{top,min} = \sqrt{gR}$

**Minimum speed at bottom:** $v_{bot,min} = \sqrt{5gR}$ (from energy conservation)

<Callout type="info">
**String vs Track Analogy**

- String ball: String can only pull → $T \geq 0$ → same minimum conditions
- Track (inside): Track can only push (normal) → $N \geq 0$ → same minimum conditions
- Rod: Can both push and pull → no minimum speed condition at top
</Callout>

**Equations:**

Vertical equilibrium: $T\cos\theta = mg$ ...(i)

Horizontal (centripetal): $T\sin\theta = \frac{mv^2}{r} = mr\omega^2$ ...(ii)

where $r = L\sin\theta$ (radius of circle).

Dividing (ii) by (i):

$$\tan\theta = \frac{r\omega^2}{g} = \frac{L\sin\theta \cdot \omega^2}{g}$$

$$\cos\theta = \frac{g}{L\omega^2}$$

<Formula label="Conical Pendulum">
$$\cos\theta = \frac{g}{L\omega^2} \qquad \omega = \sqrt{\frac{g}{L\cos\theta}}$$

$$T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L\cos\theta}{g}} \qquad v = \sqrt{gL\sin\theta\tan\theta}$$
</Formula>

**Key observations:**
- As $\omega$ increases, $\cos\theta$ decreases → $\theta$ increases (cone opens up)
- At $\omega \to \infty$: $\theta \to 90°$, string becomes horizontal
- At $\omega \to 0$: $\theta \to 0°$, pendulum hangs vertically
- Time period $T = 2\pi\sqrt{h/g}$ where $h = L\cos\theta$ is the vertical height

## SECTION 10 — Motion in a Horizontal Circle

---

### 10.1 Car on a Flat Circular Road

A car of mass $m$ moves at speed $v$ on a flat circular road of radius $R$.

Centripetal force is provided by **static friction** between tyres and road:

$$\frac{mv^2}{R} \leq \mu_s mg$$

$$v_{max} = \sqrt{\mu_s gR}$$

<Callout type="tip">
**JEE Insight**

Note: The maximum speed depends on $\mu_s$ (static), not $\mu_k$. The tyres are not sliding — they are in rolling contact with the road. The friction is static (at the contact patch).
</Callout>

When a road is banked at angle $\theta$, the normal force has a horizontal component pointing toward the centre — this helps provide centripetal force.

</Formula>

### 10.3 Banking with Friction

**Minimum speed** (car tends to slide inward → friction acts outward up the bank):

$$v_{min} = \sqrt{\frac{Rg(\tan\theta - \mu_s)}{1 + \mu_s\tan\theta}}$$

**Maximum speed** (car tends to slide outward → friction acts inward down the bank):

$$v_{max} = \sqrt{\frac{Rg(\tan\theta + \mu_s)}{1 - \mu_s\tan\theta}}$$

<Formula label="Speed Range on Banked Road with Friction">
$$\sqrt{\frac{Rg(\tan\theta - \mu)}{1 + \mu\tan\theta}} \leq v \leq \sqrt{\frac{Rg(\tan\theta + \mu)}{1 - \mu\tan\theta}}$$
</Formula>

**Derivation of $v_{max}$:**

When car tends to slide outward, friction $f = \mu_s N$ acts down the bank (inward component helps centripetal).

Vertical: $N\cos\theta - \mu_s N\sin\theta = mg \Rightarrow N = \frac{mg}{\cos\theta - \mu_s\sin\theta}$

Horizontal: $N\sin\theta + \mu_s N\cos\theta = \frac{mv^2}{R}$

$N(\sin\theta + \mu_s\cos\theta) = \frac{mv^2}{R}$

Substituting $N$:

$\frac{mg(\sin\theta + \mu_s\cos\theta)}{\cos\theta - \mu_s\sin\theta} = \frac{mv^2}{R}$

$v^2 = Rg\cdot\frac{\sin\theta + \mu_s\cos\theta}{\cos\theta - \mu_s\sin\theta} = Rg\cdot\frac{\tan\theta + \mu_s}{1 - \mu_s\tan\theta}$ ✓

### 10.4 Cyclist on Circular Road (Lean Angle)

A cyclist leans inward at angle $\theta$ to the vertical to maintain circular motion.

The resultant of normal force and friction provides centripetal force:

$$\tan\theta = \frac{v^2}{Rg}$$

This is the **lean angle** from vertical. Numerically same as banking angle for the same speed and radius.

## SECTION 11 — Circular Motion in a Vertical Plane — Advanced

---

### 11.1 Bead on a Rotating Hoop

A bead of mass $m$ slides on a smooth circular hoop (radius $R$) which rotates with angular velocity $\omega$ about a vertical diameter.

Let the bead be at angle $\theta$ from the bottom.

Forces: Weight $mg$ (down), Normal from hoop $N$ (radially inward, perpendicular to hoop surface).

For steady circular motion at angle $\theta$ (bead at fixed position on hoop):

The bead moves in a horizontal circle of radius $r = R\sin\theta$, so its centripetal acceleration is $\omega^2 R\sin\theta$, directed horizontally toward the axis.

The normal force from the wire is perpendicular to the hoop and therefore has **no component along the tangent** to the hoop. Resolving along the tangential direction (along the wire), only gravity contributes, and it must supply the tangential component of the centripetal acceleration:

$$\underbrace{mg\sin\theta}_{\text{gravity along tangent}} = \underbrace{m\omega^2 R\sin\theta\cos\theta}_{\text{tangential part of centripetal accel.}}$$

$$g\sin\theta = \omega^2 R\sin\theta\cos\theta$$

Either $\sin\theta = 0$ ($\theta = 0$, bottom) or:

$$\cos\theta = \frac{g}{\omega^2 R}$$

<Formula label="Bead on Rotating Hoop — Equilibrium">
$$\cos\theta = \frac{g}{\omega^2 R}$$

Valid only when $\omega^2 R > g$ (otherwise only $\theta = 0$ is equilibrium).
</Formula>

---

### 11.2 Particle on Inner Surface of Sphere

A particle moves on the smooth inner surface of a sphere (radius $R$) in a horizontal circle at height $h$ below the top.

The particle is at angle $\theta$ from the vertical (top). Height below top = $R - R\cos\theta = R(1-\cos\theta)$.

Normal $N$ from sphere: radially inward (toward centre of sphere).

Vertical: $N\cos\theta = mg$ ...(i)

Horizontal (centripetal): $N\sin\theta = m\omega^2 R\sin\theta$ ...(ii)

From (ii): $N = m\omega^2 R$

From (i): $m\omega^2 R\cos\theta = mg \Rightarrow \omega = \sqrt{g/(R\cos\theta)}$

Time period: $T = 2\pi/\omega = 2\pi\sqrt{R\cos\theta/g}$

## SECTION 12 — Non-Inertial Rotating Frame

---

### 12.1 Centrifugal Force — Pseudo Force in Rotating Frame

In a **rotating reference frame** (angular velocity $\omega$), every object experiences a pseudo force called the **centrifugal force**:

<Formula label="Centrifugal Force (Pseudo Force in Rotating Frame)">
$$\vec{F}_{centrifugal} = +m\omega^2 r \hat{r}$$

Direction: radially outward from the rotation axis. Magnitude: $m\omega^2 r$.
</Formula>

This is exactly equal and opposite to the centripetal force, which is why objects appear to be in equilibrium in the rotating frame.

**In rotating frame:** Object in circular motion → centrifugal force outward balances centripetal force inward → apparent equilibrium.

**In inertial frame:** Object in circular motion → real inward force (centripetal) → object accelerates toward centre.

---

### 12.2 Centrifugal Force in Conical Pendulum (Rotating Frame)

In the rotating frame of the conical pendulum:

Radially: $T\sin\theta = m\omega^2 r$ (centrifugal balances tension component)

Vertically: $T\cos\theta = mg$

Same result as before — as expected.

---

### 12.3 Coriolis Force

In a rotating frame, there is an additional pseudo force called the **Coriolis force** when an object moves within the rotating frame:

$$\vec{F}_{Coriolis} = -2m\vec{\omega} \times \vec{v}'$$

where $\vec{v}'$ is the velocity in the rotating frame.

For JEE, Coriolis force is beyond the standard syllabus but occasionally appears in JEE Advanced. It causes cyclones, trade winds, and the deflection of artillery shells.

## SECTION 13 — Advanced Applications

---

### 13.1 Death Well / Wall of Death

A motorcyclist rides inside a cylindrical wall ("death well"). The wall provides a normal force $N$ horizontally (inward = centripetal), while static friction vertically supports the rider:

Centripetal: $N = \frac{mv^2}{r}$

Vertical: $f = mg$ (friction upward balances weight)

For the rider not to slide down: $f \leq \mu_s N$

$$mg \leq \mu_s \frac{mv^2}{r} \Rightarrow v \geq \sqrt{\frac{gr}{\mu_s}}$$

**Minimum speed:**

<Formula label="Death Well (Wall of Death)">
$$v_{min} = \sqrt{\frac{gr}{\mu_s}}$$
</Formula>

---

### 13.2 Car Over a Hump (Convex Bridge)

A car of mass $m$ moves at speed $v$ over a convex bridge of radius $R$.

At the top of the hump, centripetal direction is downward (toward centre of curvature).

Newton's 2nd Law: $mg - N = \frac{mv^2}{R}$

$$N = m\left(g - \frac{v^2}{R}\right)$$

**Normal force decreases as speed increases.** At maximum speed, $N = 0$:

$$v_{max} = \sqrt{gR}$$

Above this speed, the car leaves the road.

---

### 13.3 Car in a Valley (Concave Bridge)

At the bottom of a valley of radius $R$:

$N - mg = \frac{mv^2}{R}$

$$N = m\left(g + \frac{v^2}{R}\right) > mg$$

Passenger feels heavier.

---

### 13.4 Circular Motion and Springs

A mass $m$ on a frictionless table is connected to a spring (natural length $l_0$, constant $k$) and moves in a circle of radius $r$.

Spring extension: $x = r - l_0$

Spring force = centripetal force:

$$k(r - l_0) = m\omega^2 r$$

$$r = \frac{kl_0}{k - m\omega^2}$$

(Valid for $k > m\omega^2$; if $\omega^2 = k/m$, resonance — $r \to \infty$)

## SECTION 14 — Error Analysis

---

| # | Mistake | Correct Approach |
|---|---------|-----------------|
| 1 | Treating centripetal force as a separate force in FBD | Centripetal force = net inward force from real forces; never add it separately |
| 2 | Confusing centripetal and centrifugal forces | Centripetal is real (inertial frame); centrifugal is pseudo (rotating frame only) |
| 3 | $v_{top,min} = 0$ for string | $v_{top,min} = \sqrt{gL}$ for string; 0 only for rigid rod |
| 4 | Using $\mu_k$ for maximum speed on curved road | Use $\mu_s$ (tyres don't slide — rolling contact) |
| 5 | Forgetting normal force changes at top vs bottom of circular loop | $N_{top} = mv^2/r - mg$; $N_{bot} = mv^2/r + mg$ |
| 6 | Wrong sign: at top, saying $N - mg = mv^2/r$ | At top: both $N$ and $mg$ point inward → $N + mg = mv^2/r$ |
| 7 | In banked road, ignoring friction gives wrong $v_{max}$ | Friction significantly increases $v_{max}$; always check if friction is given |
| 8 | Thinking work done by centripetal force is non-zero | Centripetal force ⊥ velocity → zero work done, kinetic energy unchanged in UCM |
| 9 | Forgetting tension at top for minimum speed = 0, not minimum $N$ | For string: $T_{min} = 0$ (string slack). For track: $N_{min} = 0$ (leaves track). Same condition. |
| 10 | Using wrong formula for conical pendulum period | $T = 2\pi\sqrt{L\cos\theta / g} = 2\pi\sqrt{h/g}$ where $h$ = vertical height |
| 11 | In conical pendulum, increasing $\omega$ increases $\theta$ — not decreasing it | As $\omega \uparrow$, $\cos\theta = g/\omega^2 L$ decreases, so $\theta \uparrow$ (opens wider) |
| 12 | Forgetting that rod can provide compression at top | At top of vertical circle with rod, normal force can be outward (rod pushes) |

## SECTION 15 — Solved Examples

---

### JEE Main Level Examples

**M1.** A stone of mass 0.5 kg is tied to a string of length 1 m and swung in a horizontal circle at 2 rev/s. Find tension and centripetal force.

**Solution:**
$\omega = 2 \times 2\pi = 4\pi$ rad/s. $v = r\omega = 1 \times 4\pi = 4\pi$ m/s.

$F_c = m\omega^2 r = 0.5 \times (4\pi)^2 \times 1 = 0.5 \times 16\pi^2 \approx \mathbf{78.9 \text{ N}}$

Tension $= F_c$ (string provides centripetal force) $= \mathbf{78.9 \text{ N}}$

---

**M2.** Find the minimum speed at the top of a vertical circle (string length 1.5 m). ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
$v_{top,min} = \sqrt{gL} = \sqrt{10 \times 1.5} = \sqrt{15} \approx \mathbf{3.87 \text{ m/s}}$

---

**M3.** A ball completes a vertical circle with string length 2 m. Find minimum speed at the bottom.

**Solution:**
$v_{bot,min} = \sqrt{5gL} = \sqrt{5 \times 10 \times 2} = \sqrt{100} = \mathbf{10 \text{ m/s}}$

---

**M4.** A car of mass 1000 kg moves at 20 m/s on a flat curved road of radius 100 m. Find the centripetal force and minimum $\mu_s$.

**Solution:**
$F_c = mv^2/r = 1000 \times 400/100 = \mathbf{4000 \text{ N}}$

$\mu_s \geq F_c/(mg) = 4000/10000 = \mathbf{0.4}$

---

**M5.** A road is banked at 30°. Find ideal speed for a curve of radius 50 m. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
$v = \sqrt{Rg\tan\theta} = \sqrt{50 \times 10 \times \tan 30°} = \sqrt{500/\sqrt{3}} = \sqrt{288.7} \approx \mathbf{17 \text{ m/s}}$

---

**M6.** A conical pendulum has string length 1 m and makes 30° with vertical. Find $\omega$ and $T$.

**Solution:**
$\omega = \sqrt{g/(L\cos\theta)} = \sqrt{10/(1 \times \cos 30°)} = \sqrt{10/0.866} = \sqrt{11.55} \approx 3.4$ rad/s

$T = 2\pi/\omega \approx 1.85$ s

Or: $T = 2\pi\sqrt{L\cos\theta/g} = 2\pi\sqrt{0.866/10} \approx \mathbf{1.85 \text{ s}}$

---

**M7.** A ball of mass 0.2 kg swings in a vertical circle (string 0.8 m). Speed at top = 4 m/s. Find tension at top and bottom. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
$T_{top} = m(v^2/L - g) = 0.2(16/0.8 - 10) = 0.2(20-10) = \mathbf{2 \text{ N}}$

By energy conservation: $v_{bot}^2 = v_{top}^2 + 4gL = 16 + 32 = 48$

$T_{bot} = m(v_{bot}^2/L + g) = 0.2(48/0.8 + 10) = 0.2(60+10) = \mathbf{14 \text{ N}}$

---

**M8.** A car passes over a convex bridge (radius 40 m) at 20 m/s. Find normal force on 1000 kg car. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
$N = m(g - v^2/R) = 1000(10 - 400/40) = 1000(10-10) = \mathbf{0 \text{ N}}$

The car is on the verge of leaving the road!

---

**M9.** Find the maximum speed a car can take a convex bridge of radius 25 m. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
$v_{max} = \sqrt{gR} = \sqrt{10 \times 25} = \sqrt{250} \approx \mathbf{15.8 \text{ m/s}}$

---

**M10.** A motorcyclist rides at 10 m/s inside a cylindrical wall of radius 5 m. Find minimum $\mu_s$. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
$\mu_s \geq gr/v^2 = 10 \times 5/100 = \mathbf{0.5}$

---

**M11.** A particle moves in a circle of radius 2 m with angular velocity increasing at $\alpha = 3$ rad/s². At $t = 2$ s (starting from rest), find total acceleration.

**Solution:**
$\omega = \alpha t = 6$ rad/s. $a_c = \omega^2 r = 36 \times 2 = 72$ m/s².

$a_t = r\alpha = 2 \times 3 = 6$ m/s².

$a_{total} = \sqrt{72^2 + 6^2} = \sqrt{5184 + 36} = \sqrt{5220} \approx \mathbf{72.2 \text{ m/s}^2}$

---

**M12.** At what angle should a conical pendulum string make with the vertical if period = 1 s? (String length 1 m, $g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
$T = 2\pi\sqrt{L\cos\theta/g}$

$1 = 2\pi\sqrt{\cos\theta/10}$

$\cos\theta = 10/(4\pi^2) \approx 10/39.5 \approx 0.253$

$\theta \approx \arccos(0.253) \approx \mathbf{75.3°}$

---

**M13.** A ball on a string (2 m) swings in a vertical circle. String breaks at $T = 25 N$. Ball mass = 0.5 kg. Find max speed at bottom. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
$T_{bot} = mv_{bot}^2/L + mg$

$25 = 0.5 v^2/2 + 0.5 \times 10 = 0.25v^2 + 5$

$v^2 = 80 \Rightarrow v = \mathbf{4\sqrt{5} \approx 8.9 \text{ m/s}}$

---

**M14.** A particle moves in a circle of radius 0.5 m. Its speed at $t = 0$ is 2 m/s and increases at 1 m/s². Find magnitude of acceleration at $t = 2$ s.

**Solution:**
$v = 2 + 1\times2 = 4$ m/s. $a_c = v^2/r = 16/0.5 = 32$ m/s². $a_t = 1$ m/s².

$a = \sqrt{32^2 + 1^2} \approx \mathbf{32.0 \text{ m/s}^2}$

---

**M15.** A road is banked at angle $\theta = \arctan(0.5)$ with $\mu_s = 0.3$. Radius = 100 m. Find $v_{min}$ and $v_{max}$. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution:**
$\tan\theta = 0.5$, $\mu = 0.3$, $R = 100$ m.

$v_{min} = \sqrt{\dfrac{Rg(\tan\theta - \mu)}{1 + \mu\tan\theta}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{100\times10\times(0.5-0.3)}{1+0.3\times0.5}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{200}{1.15}} = \sqrt{173.9} \approx \mathbf{13.2 \text{ m/s}}$

$v_{max} = \sqrt{\dfrac{Rg(\tan\theta + \mu)}{1 - \mu\tan\theta}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{100\times10\times(0.5+0.3)}{1-0.15}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{800}{0.85}} = \sqrt{941} \approx \mathbf{30.7 \text{ m/s}}$

### JEE Advanced Level Examples

**A1.** A ball (mass $m$, string length $L$) is performing vertical circular motion. Find the difference in tension at the bottom and at the top.

**Solution:**
$T_{bot} = mv_{bot}^2/L + mg$

$T_{top} = mv_{top}^2/L - mg$

$T_{bot} - T_{top} = m(v_{bot}^2 - v_{top}^2)/L + 2mg$

By energy conservation: $v_{bot}^2 - v_{top}^2 = 4gL$

$$T_{bot} - T_{top} = \frac{m \cdot 4gL}{L} + 2mg = 4mg + 2mg = \mathbf{6mg}$$

This is constant regardless of the speed! A beautiful result.

---

**A2.** A particle is given a velocity $v_0$ at the bottom of a smooth vertical circular track (radius $R$). Find the angle $\theta$ from the bottom at which the particle leaves the track, given $v_0 = \sqrt{2gR}$.

**Solution:**
The particle is on the inside of the track, so the track can only push **inward**; it leaves the moment $N = 0$.

Let $\theta$ be measured from the bottom. By energy conservation, with $v_0^2 = 2gR$:

$$v^2 = v_0^2 - 2gR(1-\cos\theta) = 2gR\cos\theta$$

The radial equation has the same form as the string result of §7.6, with the normal force $N$ in place of tension:

$$N = \frac{mv^2}{R} + mg\cos\theta$$

Substituting $v^2 = 2gR\cos\theta$:

$$N = \frac{m(2gR\cos\theta)}{R} + mg\cos\theta = 3mg\cos\theta$$

Setting $N = 0$ gives $\cos\theta = 0 \Rightarrow \theta = 90°$.

**The particle leaves the track at the side — $90°$ from the bottom**, at the level of the centre, where it momentarily has zero speed.

---

**A3.** A particle slides on a smooth sphere (radius $R$) starting from rest at the top. Find the angle at which it leaves the sphere.

**Solution:**
Let $\theta$ = angle from the top. Height descended = $R - R\cos\theta = R(1-\cos\theta)$.

$v^2 = 2gR(1-\cos\theta)$

Normal force $N$ from sphere (outward from sphere surface, radially outward from center):

Radially inward (centripetal): $mg\cos\theta - N = mv^2/R$

$N = mg\cos\theta - mv^2/R = mg\cos\theta - m \cdot 2g(1-\cos\theta)$

$N = mg\cos\theta - 2mg + 2mg\cos\theta = 3mg\cos\theta - 2mg = mg(3\cos\theta - 2)$

For $N = 0$: $3\cos\theta = 2 \Rightarrow \cos\theta = 2/3$

$$\theta = \arccos(2/3) \approx 48.2°$$

Height descended when it leaves: $R(1 - 2/3) = R/3$ from top.

---

**A4.** A ball (mass $m$) is suspended from the ceiling by a string of length $L$. It is given a horizontal push at the bottom position and just completes a vertical circle. At what angle (from the vertical) does the string become slack if the string length is the same but the ball is given speed $v_0 = \sqrt{3gL}$ (less than minimum)?

**Solution:**
String becomes slack when $T = 0$. Using the general-angle tension from §7.6 (with $\theta$ measured from the bottom):

$$T = \frac{mv_{bot}^2}{L} + mg(3\cos\theta - 2)$$

With $v_{bot}^2 = 3gL$:

$$T = \frac{m(3gL)}{L} + mg(3\cos\theta - 2) = 3mg + 3mg\cos\theta - 2mg = mg(1 + 3\cos\theta)$$

$T = 0 \Rightarrow 1 + 3\cos\theta = 0 \Rightarrow \cos\theta = -1/3$

$\theta = \arccos(-1/3) \approx 109.5°$ from bottom (which is $70.5°$ past the horizontal, in the upper half).

---

**A5.** On a rough banked road ($\theta$, $\mu$, radius $R$), find the speed at which friction is zero (no tendency to slide either way). Also find the minimum radius of curvature for a car to safely negotiate the bend at 30 m/s on a road banked at 15° with $\mu = 0.3$. ($g = 10$ m/s²)

**Solution (Part 1):** Friction is zero at ideal speed: $v = \sqrt{Rg\tan\theta}$

**Part 2:** Using $v_{max}$ formula:

$v_{max}^2 = Rg\cdot\dfrac{\tan15° + 0.3}{1 - 0.3\tan15°}$

$\tan 15° = 2 - \sqrt{3} \approx 0.268$

$v_{max}^2 = Rg\cdot\dfrac{0.268 + 0.3}{1 - 0.3\times0.268} = Rg\cdot\dfrac{0.568}{0.9196} = 0.617Rg$

For $v_{max} = 30$ m/s:

$900 = 0.617 \times R \times 10 \Rightarrow R_{min} = 900/6.17 \approx \mathbf{145.9 \text{ m}}$

### Complete Formula Sheet

<Formula label="Circular Motion — Master Formula Sheet">

**Angular velocity:** $\omega = 2\pi f = 2\pi/T$

**Linear ↔ angular:** $v = r\omega$ ; $a_t = r\alpha$ ; $a_c = r\omega^2 = v^2/r$

**Centripetal force:** $F_c = mv^2/r = mr\omega^2$

**UCM — work done by $F_c$:** Zero (always perpendicular to motion)

**Total acceleration:** $a = \sqrt{a_c^2 + a_t^2}$

**Vertical circle (string):**

- Min speed at top: $v_{top,min} = \sqrt{gL}$
- Min speed at bottom: $v_{bot,min} = \sqrt{5gL}$
- Tension difference: $T_{bot} - T_{top} = 6mg$ (always!)
- String slacks at: $\cos\theta = (v_{bot}^2 - 2gL)/(3gL)$

**Vertical circle (rod):**

- Min speed at top: $v_{top,min} = 0$
- Min speed at bottom: $v_{bot,min} = \sqrt{4gL}$

**Conical pendulum:** $\cos\theta = g/(\omega^2 L)$ ; $T = 2\pi\sqrt{L\cos\theta/g}$

**Flat circular road:** $v_{max} = \sqrt{\mu_s gR}$

**Ideal banking:** $v = \sqrt{Rg\tan\theta}$ ; $\tan\theta = v^2/(Rg)$

**Banked road (with friction):** $v_{min} = \sqrt{Rg\cdot\dfrac{\tan\theta-\mu}{1+\mu\tan\theta}}$ ; $v_{max} = \sqrt{Rg\cdot\dfrac{\tan\theta+\mu}{1-\mu\tan\theta}}$

**Convex bridge:** $N = m(g - v^2/R)$ ; leaves at $v = \sqrt{gR}$

**Concave bridge:** $N = m(g + v^2/R)$

**Death well:** $v_{min} = \sqrt{gr/\mu_s}$

**Particle leaves sphere** (start from top): $\cos\theta = 2/3$ ($\theta$ from top)

**Bead on rotating hoop:** $\cos\theta = g/(\omega^2 R)$

**Spring on table:** $r = kl_0/(k - m\omega^2)$

</Formula>

### Concept Map

**Circular Motion**

Core: $F_c = mv^2/r$ (toward centre) ← always provided by real forces

↓

Applications:
- **Vertical circle** → tension varies with position → use energy conservation + Newton's 2nd Law
- **Conical pendulum** → $T\sin\theta$ is centripetal, $T\cos\theta$ = weight
- **Banked road** → $N\sin\theta$ is centripetal (ideal) → friction adjusts for other speeds
- **Bridges** → $N$ decreases over hump, increases in valley
- **Rotating frame** → add centrifugal pseudo force outward

### Last-Minute Tips

<Callout type="tip">
**5 Key Reminders**

1. **Centripetal force is not a new force** — always identify what real force(s) provide it and write $\sum F_{inward} = mv^2/r$.

2. **Vertical circle string tension:** $T_{bot} - T_{top} = 6mg$ always. Minimum speed at bottom is $\sqrt{5gL}$, tension at bottom under minimum condition is $6mg$.

3. **Banked road friction direction** depends on speed: below ideal speed → tends to slide inward → friction points outward (up the bank); above ideal speed → tends to slide outward → friction points inward (down the bank).

4. **Conical pendulum period** is $2\pi\sqrt{h/g}$ where $h = L\cos\theta$ is the vertical height, NOT the string length. As $\omega$ increases, $h$ decreases, and so does $T$.

5. **Particle leaving a surface**: $N = 0$ condition. Solve for position and speed simultaneously using energy conservation and Newton's 2nd Law.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
**3 Marks-Killing Traps**

1. **At the TOP of a vertical circle with a string:** $T + mg = mv^2/r$ (both T and mg point inward). Students often write $T - mg = mv^2/r$ (the bottom equation) by mistake.

2. **Minimum speed for complete circle** — this is $\sqrt{5gL}$ at the **bottom**. At the **top**, it is $\sqrt{gL}$. In the exam, always state which point you're referring to.

3. **Banked road formula:** The ideal speed formula $v = \sqrt{Rg\tan\theta}$ has NO friction term. If $\mu$ is given but you're asked for "ideal speed," friction is zero at ideal speed. Don't use the $\mu$ terms in the formula.
</Callout>
