The three equations of motion are the backbone of kinematics. Most students memorise them. That is a mistake — because a derived formula is impossible to forget. Here is the full derivation starting from the definition of acceleration.

## What We Start With

**Assumption:** uniform acceleration $a$ (constant).

**Definition of acceleration:**

$$a = \frac{dv}{dt}$$

This is the *only* equation we need. Everything else follows.

## Deriving the First Equation: $v = u + at$

Rearrange the definition:

$$dv = a \, dt$$

Integrate both sides. The velocity starts at $u$ (at $t = 0$) and ends at $v$ (at time $t$):

$$\int_u^v dv = \int_0^t a \, dt$$

Since $a$ is constant, it comes out of the integral:

$$v - u = at$$

$$\boxed{v = u + at}$$

Done. This is the first equation.

## Deriving the Second Equation: $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$

Now use the definition of velocity:

$$v = \frac{ds}{dt} \implies ds = v \, dt$$

Substitute $v = u + at$:

$$ds = (u + at) \, dt$$

Integrate from $s = 0$ at $t = 0$ to $s = s$ at time $t$:

$$\int_0^s ds = \int_0^t (u + at) \, dt$$

$$s = \left[ ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \right]_0^t$$

$$\boxed{s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2}$$

## Deriving the Third Equation: $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$

We want to eliminate $t$. From the first equation:

$$t = \frac{v - u}{a}$$

Substitute into the second equation:

$$s = u \cdot \frac{v-u}{a} + \frac{1}{2}a \left(\frac{v-u}{a}\right)^2$$

$$s = \frac{u(v-u)}{a} + \frac{(v-u)^2}{2a}$$

Multiply through by $2a$:

$$2as = 2u(v-u) + (v-u)^2$$

$$2as = 2uv - 2u^2 + v^2 - 2uv + u^2$$

$$2as = v^2 - u^2$$

$$\boxed{v^2 = u^2 + 2as}$$

## The Alternative Calculus Derivation

For the third equation, there is an elegant alternative using the chain rule:

$$a = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{dv}{ds} \cdot \frac{ds}{dt} = v\frac{dv}{ds}$$

Therefore:

$$v \, dv = a \, ds$$

Integrate:

$$\int_u^v v \, dv = \int_0^s a \, ds$$

$$\frac{v^2 - u^2}{2} = as$$

$$\boxed{v^2 = u^2 + 2as}$$

This is the version preferred in JEE Advanced solutions because it is compact and general.

## Why Derivation Matters

If you know these derivations, you also know **when the equations apply**: only under constant acceleration. The moment acceleration becomes variable, these equations fail and you must use calculus directly — which is exactly what JEE Advanced tests.

Understanding the proof tells you the limits of the result. That is why first-principles derivation is non-negotiable.
