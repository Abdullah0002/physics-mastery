## 1. Elements of a Communication System

```
[Information source] → [Transmitter] → [Channel/Medium] → [Receiver] → [Destination]
```

Key components:
- **Transducer:** Converts information to electrical signal.
- **Modulator:** Superimposes signal on carrier wave.
- **Demodulator:** Extracts original signal from modulated wave.
- **Amplifier:** Strengthens weak signals.

---

## 2. Signal Types

**Analog signal:** Continuous variation with time (e.g., speech waveform).

**Digital signal:** Discrete levels (0 and 1). More noise-resistant; easier to store/process.

---

## 3. Terminology

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Bandwidth | Range of frequencies in a signal |
| Noise | Unwanted electrical disturbance |
| Attenuation | Decrease in signal strength during transmission |
| Repeater | Regenerates signal to overcome attenuation |
| SNR | Signal-to-noise ratio |

---

## 4. Modulation

Modulation = varying one property (amplitude, frequency, or phase) of a high-frequency **carrier wave** in accordance with the lower-frequency **message signal**.

**Why modulate?**
- Transmit over large distances.
- Allow multiple simultaneous transmissions (multiplexing).
- Impractical antenna size for direct transmission at audio frequencies.

---

## 5. Amplitude Modulation (AM)

The amplitude of the carrier is varied proportional to the message signal.

Carrier: $c(t) = A_c\cos(\omega_c t)$; message: $m(t) = A_m\cos(\omega_m t)$.

$$s_{AM}(t) = A_c[1 + \mu\cos(\omega_m t)]\cos(\omega_c t)$$

**Modulation index:**

<Formula label="Modulation Index (AM)">
$$\mu_a = \frac{A_m}{A_c}$$
</Formula>

For undistorted AM: $0 \leq \mu_a \leq 1$.

**Sidebands:** AM signal contains 3 frequencies: $f_c$, $f_c+f_m$, $f_c-f_m$.

**Bandwidth of AM:** $B = 2f_m$ (twice the message bandwidth).

---

## 6. Frequency Modulation (FM)

The frequency of the carrier is varied. FM is more noise-resistant than AM.

**Bandwidth (Carson's rule):** $B = 2(f_m + \Delta f)$ where $\Delta f$ = maximum frequency deviation.

FM broadcasting: 87.5–108 MHz range (wide bandwidth allows high-fidelity audio).

---

## 7. Propagation of EM Waves

| Mode | How | Frequency range | Range |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ground (surface) wave | Along Earth's surface | < 30 MHz | Short to medium |
| Sky wave | Reflected by ionosphere | 2–30 MHz (HF) | Long range |
| Space wave (LOS) | Line of sight | > 30 MHz (VHF/UHF) | Limited by horizon |

**Range of LOS transmission:**

<Formula label="Range of Antenna">
$$d = \sqrt{2Rh}$$
</Formula>

where $R$ = Earth's radius ($6.4\times10^6$ m), $h$ = antenna height.

**Total range (two antennas, heights $h_T$ and $h_R$):**

$$d = \sqrt{2Rh_T} + \sqrt{2Rh_R}$$

---

## 8. Ionosphere

Layer of ionised gases (~60–1000 km altitude). Reflects HF radio waves back to Earth (sky wave propagation). The maximum frequency reflected = **critical frequency** $f_c = 9\sqrt{N_{max}}$ Hz (where $N_{max}$ = electron number density).

---

## 9. Satellite Communication

Geostationary satellites (at ~36,000 km altitude) appear stationary relative to Earth. Used for: TV broadcasting, telephone, weather forecasting, GPS.

**Uplink:** Earth → satellite. **Downlink:** satellite → Earth. Different frequencies used to avoid interference.

---

## 10. Mobile Telephony and Internet

- **AM band:** 540 kHz – 1600 kHz
- **FM band:** 88 MHz – 108 MHz
- **TV:** VHF (47–230 MHz) and UHF (470–960 MHz)
- **Mobile/cellular:** 900 MHz, 1800 MHz, and higher bands (3G/4G/5G)
