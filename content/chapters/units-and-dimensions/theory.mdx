## 1. Physical Quantities

A **physical quantity** is anything that can be measured. All physical quantities have:
- A **numerical value** (magnitude)
- A **unit** (reference standard)

**Fundamental (base) quantities** are independent: length, mass, time, temperature, electric current, luminous intensity, amount of substance.

**Derived quantities** are combinations of fundamental ones: velocity, force, energy, etc.

## 3. Dimensional Formula

Dimensions express a physical quantity in terms of base quantities: $[M^a L^b T^c A^d \Theta^e]$.

### Key Dimensional Formulas

| Quantity | Dimensions |
|---|---|
| Force | $[MLT^{-2}]$ |
| Energy / Work | $[ML^2T^{-2}]$ |
| Power | $[ML^2T^{-3}]$ |
| Pressure / Stress | $[ML^{-1}T^{-2}]$ |
| Momentum / Impulse | $[MLT^{-1}]$ |
| Torque | $[ML^2T^{-2}]$ |
| Frequency | $[T^{-1}]$ |
| Gravitational constant $G$ | $[M^{-1}L^3T^{-2}]$ |
| Planck's constant $h$ | $[ML^2T^{-1}]$ |
| Coefficient of viscosity $\eta$ | $[ML^{-1}T^{-1}]$ |
| Surface tension | $[MT^{-2}]$ |
| Electric charge | $[AT]$ |
| Permittivity $\varepsilon_0$ | $[M^{-1}L^{-3}T^4A^2]$ |

<Callout type="important">
Torque ($[ML^2T^{-2}]$) and Work ($[ML^2T^{-2}]$) have the **same dimensions** even though they are physically different quantities. Dimensional analysis cannot distinguish them.
</Callout>

## 4. Principle of Homogeneity

Every term in a correct physical equation must have the **same dimensions**.

**Uses of dimensional analysis:**
1. Check the correctness of an equation.
2. Derive relationships between physical quantities (up to a dimensionless constant).
3. Convert units from one system to another.

---

## 5. Dimensional Analysis — Deriving Formulas

**Method:** Assume the quantity depends on certain variables. Write $Q = k\,A^a\,B^b\,C^c$ and equate dimensions on both sides.

**Example — Period of a simple pendulum:** Assume $T = k\,L^a\,g^b$.

<Step n={1}>Write dimensions: $[T] = [L^a][LT^{-2}]^b = [L^{a+b}T^{-2b}]$</Step>
<Step n={2}>Equate powers of $T$: $1 = -2b \Rightarrow b = -1/2$</Step>
<Step n={3}>Equate powers of $L$: $0 = a+b \Rightarrow a = 1/2$</Step>
<Step n={4}>Result: $T \propto \sqrt{L/g}$ (the constant $k = 2\pi$ from experiment)</Step>

<Callout type="tip">
Dimensional analysis cannot determine dimensionless constants (like $2\pi$) or distinguish between sine and cosine functions.
</Callout>

---

## 6. Limitations of Dimensional Analysis

1. Cannot determine dimensionless constants.
2. Cannot derive equations involving trigonometric/logarithmic/exponential functions.
3. Cannot determine if a sum or difference of two terms is correct.
4. Cannot identify the correct formula when multiple dimensionally consistent formulas exist.

## 7. Errors in Measurement

Every measurement has some **uncertainty (error)**.

### Types of Errors

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| Systematic | Consistent, one-directional; due to faulty instrument or method |
| Random | Unpredictable, fluctuate; reduced by averaging many readings |
| Gross (blunder) | Human mistakes; readings discarded |

### Definitions

**Absolute error:** $\Delta a = |a_{mean} - a_i|$

**Mean absolute error:** $\overline{\Delta a} = \frac{1}{n}\sum|\Delta a_i|$

**Relative (fractional) error:** $\dfrac{\overline{\Delta a}}{a_{mean}}$

**Percentage error:** $\dfrac{\overline{\Delta a}}{a_{mean}} \times 100\%$

---

## 8. Error Propagation

For derived quantities:

<Formula label="Sum/Difference — Absolute errors add">
$$z = a \pm b \Rightarrow \Delta z = \Delta a + \Delta b$$
</Formula>

<Formula label="Product/Quotient — Relative errors add">
$$z = \frac{a^m b^n}{c^p} \Rightarrow \frac{\Delta z}{z} = m\frac{\Delta a}{a} + n\frac{\Delta b}{b} + p\frac{\Delta c}{c}$$
</Formula>

<Callout type="important">
**Maximum possible error** — always add errors (never subtract). This gives the worst-case scenario. The actual error is usually smaller.
</Callout>

**Example — Volume of a sphere:** $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$

$$\frac{\Delta V}{V} = 3\frac{\Delta r}{r}$$

If radius has 2% error, volume has 6% error.

---

## 9. Significant Figures

Rules:
1. All non-zero digits are significant.
2. Zeros **between** non-zero digits are significant (e.g., 1005 → 4 sig figs).
3. Leading zeros are **not** significant (0.004 → 1 sig fig).
4. Trailing zeros **after** the decimal are significant (3.00 → 3 sig figs).

**Rounding in calculations:**
- **Addition/Subtraction:** Result has the same number of decimal places as the least precise number.
- **Multiplication/Division:** Result has the same number of significant figures as the least precise number.
