I have taught Physics to over 12,000 students. The ones who underperform are not the ones who studied less. They are the ones who studied **wrong**. Here are the five patterns I see destroy promising students.

## Mistake 1: Memorising Formulas Instead of Deriving Them

When a student memorises $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ without knowing where it comes from, they will forget it under exam pressure — or worse, misapply it.

When a student has derived it from $v = u + at$ and $s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$, the formula is part of their mental model. It cannot be forgotten because it is understood.

**Fix:** Before solving a single problem, derive every formula in the chapter from first principles. It takes one extra hour and saves ten.

## Mistake 2: Passive Reading

Reading your Physics textbook and feeling like you understand it is not the same as understanding it. The brain is wired to confuse *familiarity* with *knowledge*.

The only test of whether you know something is whether you can solve a problem you have never seen before.

**Fix:** After reading each concept, close the book and write down: (a) the physical principle in one sentence, (b) the governing equation, (c) what each symbol means, (d) a situation where you would use it.

## Mistake 3: Skipping Free Body Diagrams

Every mechanics problem that takes more than 90 seconds to solve is a problem where the student skipped drawing a proper free body diagram.

Newton's laws require you to identify all forces on a body. If you draw the diagram, you cannot miss a force. If you do not, you will.

**Fix:** Make it a rule: **no mechanics problem without an FBD**. Non-negotiable.

## Mistake 4: Solving Familiar Questions Only

Students solve the same types of questions until they feel confident. Then they enter the exam and fail on questions that look slightly different.

JEE is not testing your ability to replicate solutions. It is testing whether you understand the principle well enough to apply it in **unfamiliar contexts**.

**Fix:** After mastering a chapter, find questions that mix it with another chapter. Projectile + relative motion. Work-energy + circular motion. The unexpected combination is exactly what JEE asks.

## Mistake 5: No Error Analysis After Mocks

Most students look at their score after a mock test and feel good or bad. Almost none of them do what actually matters: a forensic analysis of every wrong answer.

For each error, ask:
- Was it a conceptual gap?
- Was it a formula error?
- Was it a calculation mistake?
- Was it a time management failure?

Each category requires a different fix. Without knowing which bucket your errors fall in, you will keep repeating the same pattern.

**Fix:** Spend at least as much time on post-mock analysis as on the mock itself. Keep an error log. Review it before every subsequent mock.

---

## The Common Thread

All five mistakes share the same root cause: **optimising for the feeling of productivity instead of actual learning**. Reading feels productive. Copying notes feels productive. Solving familiar problems feels productive.

Deriving from scratch, blank-page recall, and error analysis are uncomfortable. They are also what work.
