## 1. Basic Terminology

- **System:** The region under study.
- **Surroundings:** Everything outside the system.
- **State variables:** $P$, $V$, $T$, $n$ — describe thermodynamic state.
- **Process:** Change from one state to another.

---

## 2. Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics

If system A is in thermal equilibrium with system B, and B with C, then A and C are also in equilibrium. This defines **temperature**.

---

## 3. First Law of Thermodynamics

<Formula label="First Law">
$$\Delta U = Q - W$$
</Formula>

- $Q$ = heat absorbed by the system
- $W$ = work done **by** the system
- $\Delta U$ = change in internal energy

**Work done by gas:** $W = \int P\,dV$ = area under $P$–$V$ diagram.

<Callout type="important">
Sign convention: $Q$ positive = heat absorbed; $W$ positive = work done BY the gas. Some textbooks use $W$ = work done ON the gas (then $\Delta U = Q + W$). JEE uses $\Delta U = Q - W$ (work done by gas).
</Callout>

---

## 4. Thermodynamic Processes

### Isothermal (constant $T$)

$\Delta U = 0$ (ideal gas), so $Q = W$.

$$W = nRT\ln\frac{V_f}{V_i} = nRT\ln\frac{P_i}{P_f}$$

On $P$–$V$ diagram: hyperbola ($PV = \text{const}$).

### Adiabatic (no heat exchange, $Q=0$)

$\Delta U = -W$, so work done at the expense of internal energy.

$$PV^\gamma = \text{const}, \quad TV^{\gamma-1} = \text{const}, \quad T P^{-\gamma/(\gamma-1)} = \text{const}$$

<Formula label="Work in adiabatic process">
$$W = \frac{P_1V_1 - P_2V_2}{\gamma-1} = \frac{nR(T_1-T_2)}{\gamma-1}$$
</Formula>

### Isochoric (constant $V$, $W=0$)

$$Q = \Delta U = nC_V\Delta T$$

### Isobaric (constant $P$)

$$Q = nC_P\Delta T, \quad W = P\Delta V = nR\Delta T$$

---

## 5. Comparison of Processes

| Process | Condition | $W$ | $Q$ | $\Delta U$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Isothermal | $T = c$ | $nRT\ln(V_f/V_i)$ | = $W$ | 0 |
| Adiabatic | $Q = 0$ | $nR\Delta T/(\gamma-1)$ | 0 | = $-W$ |
| Isochoric | $V = c$ | 0 | $nC_V\Delta T$ | = $Q$ |
| Isobaric | $P = c$ | $P\Delta V$ | $nC_P\Delta T$ | $nC_V\Delta T$ |

---

## 6. Slope of P–V Diagram

- **Isothermal:** slope $= -P/V$
- **Adiabatic:** slope $= -\gamma P/V$

Since $\gamma > 1$, the adiabatic curve is **steeper** than the isothermal at the same point.

---

## 7. Second Law of Thermodynamics

**Kelvin–Planck statement:** No heat engine can convert all heat into work in a cyclic process. There must be a heat sink.

**Clausius statement:** Heat cannot spontaneously flow from a cold body to a hot body.

Both are equivalent.

---

## 8. Heat Engine Efficiency

$$\eta = 1 - \frac{Q_C}{Q_H} = \frac{W}{Q_H}$$

where $Q_H$ = heat absorbed from hot reservoir, $Q_C$ = heat rejected to cold reservoir.

---

## 9. Carnot Cycle

The most efficient cycle operating between temperatures $T_H$ and $T_C$:

1. Isothermal expansion at $T_H$ (absorbs $Q_H$)
2. Adiabatic expansion ($T_H \to T_C$)
3. Isothermal compression at $T_C$ (rejects $Q_C$)
4. Adiabatic compression ($T_C \to T_H$)

<Formula label="Carnot Efficiency">
$$\eta_{Carnot} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H}$$
</Formula>

<Callout type="important">
The Carnot efficiency is the **maximum** efficiency any engine can achieve between $T_H$ and $T_C$. No real engine equals it (due to irreversibilities). It depends only on temperatures, not the working substance.
</Callout>

---

## 10. Coefficient of Performance (Refrigerator)

$$\text{COP} = \frac{Q_C}{W} = \frac{Q_C}{Q_H - Q_C} = \frac{T_C}{T_H - T_C} \quad \text{(Carnot)}$$

---

## 11. Entropy

$$dS = \frac{dQ_{rev}}{T}$$

For a reversible process: $\Delta S = 0$ (cyclic). For irreversible: $\Delta S > 0$.

**Second Law (entropy form):** The entropy of an isolated system never decreases.

---

## 12. Key Formulas Summary

| Process | PV relation | Work |
|---|---|---|
| Isothermal | $PV = c$ | $nRT\ln(V_f/V_i)$ |
| Adiabatic | $PV^\gamma = c$ | $nR\Delta T/(\gamma-1)$ |
| Isobaric | $V/T = c$ | $P\Delta V$ |
| Isochoric | $P/T = c$ | 0 |

$$\eta_{Carnot} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H}, \quad \text{COP}_{Carnot} = \frac{T_C}{T_H-T_C}$$
