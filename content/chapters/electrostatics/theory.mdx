## Introduction

Electrostatics deals with **electric charges at rest** and the forces, fields, and potentials they create. It is the highest-weightage topic in Class 12 Physics for JEE, carrying approximately 9% of marks.

<Callout type="tip">
Electrostatics is heavily conceptual. Focus on the physical meaning of each quantity — rushing straight to formula-plugging leads to errors in multi-step problems.
</Callout>

---

## 1. Electric Charge

Charge is a fundamental property of matter. The SI unit is the **coulomb (C)**.

- Proton: $q = +1.6 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$
- Electron: $q = -1.6 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$

**Properties:**
1. **Quantisation:** Charge exists in integer multiples of $e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}$
2. **Conservation:** Net charge of an isolated system is constant
3. **Additivity:** Total charge = algebraic sum of all charges

---

## 2. Coulomb's Law

The electrostatic force between two **point charges** $q_1$ and $q_2$ separated by distance $r$:

<Formula label="Coulomb's Law">
$$F = k\frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\cdot\frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}$$
</Formula>

where $k = 9 \times 10^9\ \text{N m}^2 \text{C}^{-2}$ and $\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}\ \text{C}^2 \text{N}^{-1}\text{m}^{-2}$.

<Callout type="important">
Coulomb's law is valid for **point charges** or spherically symmetric charge distributions. For the force to be repulsive, charges must have the same sign; attractive for opposite signs.
</Callout>

### Vector Form

$$\vec{F}_{12} = k\frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}\hat{r}_{12}$$

where $\hat{r}_{12}$ is the unit vector from $q_1$ to $q_2$.

**Superposition Principle:** The net force on a charge is the **vector sum** of forces due to all other charges.

---

## 3. Electric Field

The electric field $\vec{E}$ at a point is the force per unit **positive** test charge:

<Formula label="Electric Field Definition">
$$\vec{E} = \frac{\vec{F}}{q_0} = k\frac{Q}{r^2}\hat{r}$$
</Formula>

### Electric Field Due to Common Distributions

| Distribution | Location | $E$ |
|---|---|---|
| Point charge $Q$ | Distance $r$ | $kQ/r^2$ |
| Infinite line charge (linear density $\lambda$) | Distance $r$ | $\lambda/(2\pi\varepsilon_0 r)$ |
| Infinite plane (surface density $\sigma$) | Any point | $\sigma/(2\varepsilon_0)$ |
| Uniformly charged sphere (total charge $Q$) | Outside ($r > R$) | $kQ/r^2$ |
| Uniformly charged sphere | Inside ($r < R$) | $kQr/R^3$ |
| Conducting sphere | Inside | $0$ |

<Callout type="derivation">
The electric field inside a conductor is always **zero** in electrostatic equilibrium. Free electrons redistribute until the internal field is cancelled. This is the basis of **electrostatic shielding**.
</Callout>

---

## 4. Electric Field Lines

- Start on positive charges, end on negative charges
- **Never cross** (the field is unique at every point)
- Density of lines indicates field strength
- Perpendicular to conductor surfaces

---

## 5. Gauss's Law

The total electric flux through any **closed surface** (Gaussian surface) equals the enclosed charge divided by $\varepsilon_0$:

<Formula label="Gauss's Law">
$$\Phi_E = \oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{Q_{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}$$
</Formula>

### Applying Gauss's Law

Choose a Gaussian surface that exploits the **symmetry** of the charge distribution:

- **Spherical symmetry** → spherical Gaussian surface
- **Cylindrical symmetry** → cylindrical Gaussian surface
- **Planar symmetry** → pillbox (flat cylinder) Gaussian surface

<Step n={1}>Identify the symmetry of the charge distribution</Step>
<Step n={2}>Choose a Gaussian surface where $\vec{E}$ is either constant or zero on each part</Step>
<Step n={3}>Calculate $\Phi_E = E \cdot A$ (since $E$ is constant on the surface)</Step>
<Step n={4}>Set equal to $Q_{enc}/\varepsilon_0$ and solve for $E$</Step>

## 6. Electric Potential

The electric potential $V$ at a point is the work done per unit positive charge to bring it from infinity to that point:

<Formula label="Electric Potential (Point Charge)">
$$V = k\frac{Q}{r} = \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r}$$
</Formula>

### Relation between $E$ and $V$

$$\vec{E} = -\nabla V = -\frac{dV}{dr}\hat{r}$$

In one dimension: $E = -\frac{dV}{dx}$

<Callout type="important">
$E$ is related to the **gradient** of $V$. If $V$ is constant in a region, $E = 0$ there. The direction of $\vec{E}$ is from high potential to low potential.
</Callout>

### Potential Due to Multiple Charges

$$V = k\sum_i \frac{q_i}{r_i}$$

Potential is a **scalar** — just add algebraically (no vectors!).

### Equipotential Surfaces

- Surfaces of constant potential
- **Perpendicular** to electric field lines
- No work done moving a charge along an equipotential surface

---

## 7. Electric Potential Energy

The potential energy stored when charge $q$ is at potential $V$:

<Formula label="Potential Energy">
$$U = qV$$
</Formula>

For a system of two point charges:

<Formula label="Two-Charge Potential Energy">
$$U = k\frac{q_1 q_2}{r}$$
</Formula>

**Work-energy theorem for electric charges:**

$$W_{external} = \Delta U = q(V_f - V_i)$$

$$W_{electric\ force} = -\Delta U = q(V_i - V_f)$$

---

## 8. Conductors in Electrostatic Equilibrium

1. $\vec{E} = 0$ inside the conductor
2. All excess charge resides on the **surface**
3. $\vec{E}$ is perpendicular to the surface at every point
4. Surface is an equipotential
5. Electric field just outside: $E = \sigma/\varepsilon_0$

---

## 9. Capacitance

A capacitor stores charge. The capacitance $C$ is defined as:

<Formula label="Capacitance">
$$C = \frac{Q}{V}$$
</Formula>

The SI unit is the **farad (F)**.

### Parallel Plate Capacitor

<Formula label="Parallel Plate Capacitor">
$$C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d}$$
</Formula>

With dielectric of constant $\kappa$:

$$C = \frac{\kappa\varepsilon_0 A}{d}$$

### Energy Stored in a Capacitor

<Formula label="Energy in Capacitor">
$$U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{Q^2}{2C} = \frac{QV}{2}$$
</Formula>

### Combinations

**Series:** $\frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} + \cdots$

**Parallel:** $C_{eq} = C_1 + C_2 + \cdots$

<Callout type="tip">
Capacitors in series have **equal charge**; capacitors in parallel have **equal voltage**. This is the dual of resistors (series = equal current, parallel = equal voltage).
</Callout>

## 10. JEE Advanced Concepts

### Charge Distribution on Connected Conductors

When two conducting spheres of radii $r_1$ and $r_2$ are connected by a wire, they reach the same potential:

$$V_1 = V_2 \Rightarrow \frac{q_1}{r_1} = \frac{q_2}{r_2}$$

Charge distributes in the ratio $q_1 : q_2 = r_1 : r_2$.

### Electric Dipole

A dipole consists of $-q$ and $+q$ separated by distance $2a$. Dipole moment: $p = q \cdot 2a$.

$$E_{axial} = \frac{2kp}{r^3}, \quad E_{equatorial} = \frac{kp}{r^3}$$

Torque on dipole in uniform field: $\tau = pE\sin\theta$

Energy: $U = -pE\cos\theta = -\vec{p}\cdot\vec{E}$

<Callout type="important">
At the axial position, $E = 2kp/r^3$; at the equatorial position, $E = kp/r^3$. The axial field is exactly twice the equatorial field at the same distance.
</Callout>
