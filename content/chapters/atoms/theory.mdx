## 1. Atomic Models — Historical

### Thomson's Plum-Pudding Model
Electrons embedded in a diffuse positive charge. Disproved by Rutherford's experiment.

### Rutherford's Nuclear Model (1911)

**α-scattering experiment:** A beam of α-particles aimed at a thin gold foil showed:
- Most passed straight through.
- A few deflected at large angles.
- Very few (1 in 8000) bounced back.

**Conclusion:** The atom is mostly empty space; positive charge + most mass concentrated in a tiny **nucleus**; electrons orbit around it.

**Limitation:** Classical orbits would cause electrons to spiral inward (radiating energy) — atom would collapse in ~$10^{-8}$ s. Rutherford's model could not explain atomic stability.

### Bohr's Model (1913)

Three postulates:
<Step n={1}>Electrons move in circular orbits (stationary states) without radiating.</Step>
<Step n={2}>Angular momentum is quantised: $L = mvr = nh/(2\pi) = n\hbar$.</Step>
<Step n={3}>Radiation emitted/absorbed as electron transitions between levels: $E_{photon} = |E_i - E_f|$.</Step>

## 2. Bohr Model Results for Hydrogen ($Z=1$)

<Formula label="Radius of nth orbit">
$$r_n = \frac{n^2 a_0}{Z}, \quad a_0 = 0.529\ \text{Å}$$
</Formula>

<Formula label="Speed in nth orbit">
$$v_n = \frac{Z\alpha c}{n} = \frac{2.18\times10^6 Z}{n}\ \text{m/s}$$
</Formula>

<Formula label="Energy of nth level">
$$E_n = -\frac{13.6\,Z^2}{n^2}\ \text{eV}$$
</Formula>

**For hydrogen:** $E_1 = -13.6$ eV, $E_2 = -3.4$ eV, $E_3 = -1.51$ eV, …

**Ionisation energy:** Energy to remove electron from $n=1$: $13.6$ eV.

## 3. Hydrogen Spectrum — Spectral Series

<Formula label="Rydberg Formula">
$$\frac{1}{\lambda} = R_H Z^2\left(\frac{1}{n_1^2} - \frac{1}{n_2^2}\right), \quad R_H = 1.097\times10^7\ \text{m}^{-1}$$
</Formula>

| Series | Transition ($n_2 \to n_1$) | Region | $n_1$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lyman | $n\to1$ | Ultraviolet | 1 |
| Balmer | $n\to2$ | Visible | 2 |
| Paschen | $n\to3$ | Infrared | 3 |
| Brackett | $n\to4$ | Far IR | 4 |
| Pfund | $n\to5$ | Far IR | 5 |

**Number of lines** when electron falls from level $n$ to 1: $n(n-1)/2$.

## 4. Hydrogen-Like Ions

For ions with nuclear charge $Z$ and one electron (He$^+$, Li$^{2+}$, etc.):

$$E_n = -\frac{13.6\,Z^2}{n^2}\ \text{eV}, \quad r_n = \frac{n^2 a_0}{Z}$$

**Example:** He$^+$ ($Z=2$): $E_1 = -54.4$ eV, $r_1 = 0.265$ Å.

---

## 5. Excitation and Ionisation

**Excitation energy:** Energy needed to raise electron from ground state to higher level.

$$E_{exc} = E_n - E_1 = 13.6\left(1 - \frac{1}{n^2}\right)\ \text{eV}$$

**Ionisation energy** from $n$th level:

$$E_{ion,n} = \frac{13.6}{n^2}\ \text{eV}$$

---

## 6. Limitations of Bohr's Model

1. Cannot explain spectra of multi-electron atoms.
2. Cannot explain fine structure of spectral lines (spin, relativistic effects).
3. No explanation for relative intensities of spectral lines.
4. Fails for molecules.
5. The concept of a "definite orbit" contradicts the uncertainty principle.

## 7. Quantum Numbers (Beyond Bohr)

In the full quantum mechanical treatment:

| Quantum number | Symbol | Values | Physical meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| Principal | $n$ | 1, 2, 3, … | Shell, energy |
| Azimuthal | $l$ | 0 to $n-1$ | Orbital angular momentum |
| Magnetic | $m_l$ | $-l$ to $+l$ | Orientation of orbital |
| Spin | $m_s$ | $+1/2$, $-1/2$ | Electron spin |
