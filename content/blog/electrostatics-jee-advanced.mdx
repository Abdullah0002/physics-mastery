Electrostatics is the most concept-dense chapter in Class 12 Physics. JEE Advanced tests it at a depth that surprises even well-prepared students. Here are the ten concepts that matter most.

## 1. Superposition — Add Fields as Vectors, Potentials as Scalars

The electric field at any point is the **vector** sum of fields from all charges:

$$\vec{E}_{total} = \sum_i \vec{E}_i$$

The potential is a **scalar** sum:

$$V_{total} = \sum_i \frac{kq_i}{r_i}$$

Always work with potential when symmetry breaks vector addition — it is far faster.

## 2. Gauss's Law: The Right Surface Matters

$$\oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{Q_{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}$$

Gauss's law is always true, but only **useful** when the charge distribution is symmetric enough that $E$ is constant over your chosen surface. For a sphere, cylinder, or infinite plane — choose accordingly.

## 3. The Relationship Between $E$ and $V$

$$E = -\frac{dV}{dr}$$

The electric field points in the direction of **decreasing** potential. A region of uniform potential (like the surface of a conductor) has **zero** electric field component parallel to it.

## 4. Conductors in Equilibrium: 5 Rules

1. $E = 0$ inside a conductor
2. All charge resides on the **surface**
3. The surface is an **equipotential**
4. $E$ just outside the surface $= \sigma/\varepsilon_0$, perpendicular to surface
5. Inside a cavity with no charge: $E = 0$, even if the conductor is charged

## 5. Energy Stored in a Capacitor

$$U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{Q^2}{2C} = \frac{1}{2}QV$$

When two capacitors are connected, the final energy is **less** than the initial energy. The difference is dissipated as heat (or radiated) — **regardless** of whether there is a resistor in the circuit.

## 6. Energy Density of the Electric Field

$$u = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2 \quad \text{(J/m}^3\text{)}$$

This lets you calculate energy stored in any region by integration. Key for problems involving changing capacitor geometry.

## 7. Dielectrics: Polarisation and Bound Charges

When a dielectric is inserted into a capacitor:
- $C \to KC$ (increases by dielectric constant $K$)
- $E \to E/K$ inside (field is reduced)
- Bound surface charges appear on the dielectric

JEE Advanced frequently asks about the force on a dielectric being pulled into a capacitor. Use energy methods, not force formulas.

## 8. The Electric Dipole — Near-field vs Far-field

For an electric dipole $p = qd$:

$$E_{axial} = \frac{2kp}{r^3}, \quad E_{equatorial} = \frac{kp}{r^3}$$

Note: the equatorial field **opposes** the dipole direction. A key sign error catches many students.

## 9. Charge Distribution on Concentric Shells

Charge distributes such that the **net field inside every conductor is zero**. For nested shells, work outward, applying the conductor rules at each layer. The innermost shell's charge always induces equal and opposite charge on the inner surface of the next shell.

## 10. Method of Images (JEE Advanced Only)

A point charge $q$ at distance $d$ from a grounded infinite conducting plane behaves as if there is a charge $-q$ at the mirror position. The force of attraction is:

$$F = \frac{kq^2}{(2d)^2}$$

This appears in JEE Advanced roughly once every three years.
